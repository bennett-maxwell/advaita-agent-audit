# Franchise Resale Opportunities in 2026: How to Buy an Existing Franchise and What to Watch For

Not every franchise opportunity involves building something from scratch. In 2026, one of the most overlooked — and potentially most attractive — paths into franchise ownership is the resale market: buying an existing franchise location from its current owner rather than opening a brand-new unit.

Franchise resales come with real advantages: established cash flow, a trained staff, an existing customer base, and a shorter ramp to profitability. But they also come with risks that new unit purchases do not. This guide walks through everything a prospective buyer needs to know about the franchise resale market, including how to find opportunities, how to value them, what due diligence looks like, and how [FKI (Franchise Knowledge Institute)](https://franchiseki.com) helps buyers evaluate resale versus new unit options.

## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale occurs when an existing franchisee sells their operating business to a new owner. Unlike buying a new franchise unit directly from the franchisor, a resale transaction involves purchasing a going concern — a business that is already operating, generating revenue, and employing staff.

The seller might be exiting for any number of reasons:
- Retirement or life transition
- Health or personal circumstances
- Desire to liquidate equity and pursue other opportunities
- Relocation
- Burnout or operational challenges they haven't been able to resolve

The reason for sale matters enormously during due diligence. A retiring owner selling a thriving, profitable location is a very different proposition from a struggling operator trying to exit a unit that has never found its footing.

## Resale vs. New Unit: The Key Differences

Understanding the trade-offs between buying a resale and opening a new unit is essential before entering either process.

### Advantages of Buying a Franchise Resale

**Existing Cash Flow:** A resale that is generating positive cash flow means you may begin recouping your investment from day one rather than enduring a ramp period of 6 to 18 months or more that is typical with new units.

**Established Customer Base:** Loyal, repeat customers are among the most valuable and hardest-to-build assets in any retail or service business. A resale delivers this customer relationship equity immediately.

**Trained Staff:** Existing employees who know the brand's systems, culture, and customers reduce your operational burden during the transition period.

**Shorter Path to Profitability:** Because the location is operational and generating revenue, buyers avoid the cash burn of the startup phase that consumes working capital in new unit openings.

**Negotiable Price:** Unlike a new franchise where the initial investment is largely dictated by the FDD, resale prices are negotiable between buyer and seller.

### Disadvantages of Buying a Franchise Resale

**Inheriting Problems:** A resale may come with deferred maintenance, underperforming staff, strained supplier relationships, or a damaged local reputation that is expensive to repair.

**Less Franchisor Support During Onboarding:** Some franchisors provide extensive grand-opening support for new franchisees but less structured support for resale buyers.

**Higher Purchase Price for Strong Units:** A profitable, well-run resale will command a premium over the cost of opening a new unit. Buyers pay for the established cash flow.

**Limited Availability:** You cannot simply choose to buy a resale in your preferred market — you are limited to what existing owners are willing to sell.

## How to Value a Franchise Resale

The most common valuation method for small business franchise resales is a multiple of Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE).

**Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)** represents the total financial benefit to a single owner-operator: net profit plus the owner's salary, plus any personal expenses run through the business, plus depreciation and amortization, plus one-time or non-recurring expenses.

Typical SDE multiples for franchise resales in 2026 range from **2x to 4x SDE**, depending on:

- Industry and concept (service businesses typically trade at lower multiples than high-growth concepts)
- Location and market demographics
- Lease terms and years remaining
- Transferability of key staff
- Trend in revenue (growing, stable, or declining)
- Franchisor's reputation and brand health

For example, a franchise location with $150,000 in annual SDE might sell for $300,000 to $600,000, with the actual price determined through negotiation informed by comparable sales.

In addition to the business value, buyers must also consider:
- **Lease assignment or renegotiation costs**
- **Franchisor transfer fees** (disclosed in the FDD, typically $5,000 to $25,000)
- **Working capital** to fund operations through the transition
- **Lender fees** if financing the acquisition

## Due Diligence for Franchise Resales

Due diligence on a resale is more complex than on a new unit and should involve a franchise-experienced attorney and accountant. Key areas include:

**Financial Review**
- Three to five years of profit and loss statements
- Tax returns (to verify P&Ls against reported income)
- Monthly revenue trends — identify seasonality and any recent decline
- Accounts payable and receivable
- Payroll records and employee costs

**Operational Review**
- Franchisor's performance reports and audit history for the unit
- Mystery shop reports if available
- Online review profile (Google, Yelp) — look for trends, not just current rating
- Current staffing and any anticipated departures post-sale

**Legal and Compliance Review**
- Current franchise agreement — transfer provisions, remaining term, renewal rights
- Lease agreement — term remaining, assignability, rent increases, exclusivity clauses
- Any outstanding litigation or franchisor-issued default notices
- Permits, licenses, and health/safety compliance history

**Franchisor Approval**
The franchisor must approve any transfer. They will conduct their own review of the buyer's qualifications, require completion of their training program, and may have a right of first refusal on the purchase. Never complete a resale transaction without franchisor approval in writing.

## Red Flags in Franchise Resales

Not every resale is a good deal. Watch for these warning signs:

- **Declining revenue trend** over two or more consecutive years without a clear, verifiable explanation
- **Owner unwilling to provide tax returns** or insisting on cash-basis financials only
- **Short lease term** with no renewal option and an unwilling landlord
- **High staff turnover** or reports of poor franchisor relationships
- **Pending litigation** between the current franchisee and the franchisor
- **Seller rushing the timeline** — legitimate sellers allow proper due diligence
- **Discrepancy between reported P&L and actual tax filings**

## Frequently Asked Questions About Franchise Resales

**Q: Is it cheaper to buy a franchise resale than open a new unit?**
Not always. A profitable, well-established resale may cost significantly more than opening a new unit in the same brand. However, the premium is often justified by the immediate cash flow and reduced startup risk. Underperforming resales may sell below the cost of a new unit, but they carry corresponding operational risks.

**Q: Does the franchisor have to approve a resale transfer?**
Yes. Every franchise agreement includes transfer provisions requiring franchisor approval. The franchisor will review the buyer's qualifications, require training completion, and may charge a transfer fee. Some agreements include a right of first refusal allowing the franchisor to purchase the unit themselves before approving an outside sale.

**Q: Can I get SBA financing for a franchise resale?**
Yes. SBA 7(a) loans are commonly used to finance franchise resales. Lenders will require the business's financial records, a business appraisal, and the buyer's personal financial documentation. SBA financing typically covers up to 85% of the acquisition cost for qualifying transactions.

**Q: How long does a franchise resale transaction typically take?**
From signed letter of intent to closing, most franchise resale transactions take 60 to 120 days. Delays often occur in the franchisor approval process or due diligence review. Buyers should not attempt to accelerate this timeline — thorough due diligence protects your investment.
