# How to Read a Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) in 2026

The Franchise Disclosure Document is the single most important piece of paper in franchising. Before you sign a franchise agreement or pay any fees, federal law requires the franchisor to give you an FDD. Most buyers receive it, set it on their desk, and feel immediately overwhelmed by 200+ pages of dense legal language.

This guide cuts through the complexity. You will learn what an FDD is, which of the 23 items demand your closest attention, and how to compare FDDs across brands like a professional buyer.

## What Is an FDD?

The Franchise Disclosure Document is a legal document mandated by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) under the Franchise Rule. It standardizes the information that franchisors must provide to prospective buyers before any transaction occurs. Every FDD follows the same 23-item structure, which makes comparison across brands possible.

The FDD is not a sales document. It is a legal disclosure. The information in it is regulated, and franchisors can face legal liability for material misrepresentations. That said, the way information is *presented* can still vary widely — which is why knowing how to read it matters.

**Important:** You must receive the FDD at least 14 calendar days before signing any agreement or making any payment to the franchisor. This cooling-off period exists to protect you.

## The 23 Items: Which Ones Matter Most

All 23 items have their purpose, but experienced franchise buyers and attorneys focus their attention on a core set.

### Item 3: Litigation History

This item discloses any pending or prior lawsuits involving the franchisor, its affiliates, directors, or officers. A long history of litigation with franchisees is a serious warning sign. Some litigation is inevitable in a large system — but patterns of franchisee-initiated lawsuits, particularly about fraud or misrepresentation, deserve deep investigation.

### Item 5 and Item 6: Fees

Item 5 covers the initial franchise fee. Item 6 covers all other fees — including royalties, marketing fund contributions, technology fees, training fees, and renewal fees. Read Item 6 carefully because the headline royalty rate rarely tells the full cost story. Technology fees and brand fund contributions can add 2–4% on top of a stated royalty rate.

### Item 7: Estimated Initial Investment

Item 7 presents a table of estimated costs to open the franchise. This includes the franchise fee, equipment, real estate, working capital, and other startup costs. Pay attention to the low and high estimates — the high estimate is often more realistic. Also note what the working capital estimate covers (typically 3–6 months of operating expenses).

### Item 12: Territory

Item 12 describes what territorial rights you will receive — whether they are exclusive, protected, or non-exclusive — and how territory is defined (by zip code, radius, population, or county). This item also describes what rights, if any, the franchisor retains to sell competing products or services in your territory through other channels.

### Item 19: Financial Performance Representations

This is the most analyzed item in any FDD. Item 19 is where a franchisor *may* choose to disclose financial performance data for existing locations. The key word is *may* — it is voluntary.

When reading Item 19, ask:
- Is this gross revenue or net income?
- What percentage of franchisees reported?
- Is this based on franchisee-owned or company-owned locations?
- What is the median vs. the average?
- What time period does this cover?

A strong Item 19 shows a range of performance across different types of units, includes median figures, and clearly distinguishes gross revenue from net income. A weak or absent Item 19 should prompt you to ask why the franchisor is not sharing performance data.

### Item 20: Outlets and Franchisee Information

Item 20 is a goldmine of system health data. It shows how many locations opened, closed, transferred, or were terminated in each of the last three years. A system with a high closure rate or lots of terminations deserves scrutiny. It also provides contact information for current and former franchisees — **always call them**.

### Item 21: Audited Financial Statements

Item 21 contains the franchisor's audited financial statements for the past three years. This tells you whether the franchisor itself is financially healthy. Look for consistent revenue growth, positive operating income, and reasonable debt levels. A financially weak franchisor may not be able to support its franchisee network long-term.

## How to Compare FDDs Across Brands

Once you have FDDs from multiple brands, the 23-item structure makes comparison systematic. Build a comparison spreadsheet:

- **Item 5:** Initial franchise fee
- **Item 6:** Total fee load (royalty + marketing + technology)
- **Item 7:** Total estimated initial investment (high estimate)
- **Item 12:** Territory type and size
- **Item 19:** Net income median (if disclosed)
- **Item 20:** Closure rate (closures / total units)
- **Item 21:** Franchisor revenue trend

This side-by-side view lets you evaluate which brand offers the best combination of value, support, and financial performance for your investment level.

## Why You Need a Franchise Attorney

The FDD is a disclosure document. The **franchise agreement** is the contract you actually sign — and it is not the same document. The franchise agreement governs your actual rights and obligations and is often heavily weighted in the franchisor's favor.

A franchise attorney — not a general business attorney, but one who specializes in franchise law — will:
- Review the franchise agreement for unusual restrictions
- Flag non-standard clauses in the FDD
- Compare terms to industry norms
- Identify personal guarantee exposure
- Review renewal and termination conditions

Expect to pay $1,500–$3,500 for a qualified franchise attorney review. This is one of the best investments a franchise buyer can make.

## What Litigation History Tells You

Item 3 discloses pending or concluded actions against the franchisor, its officers, and directors in the past 10 years. Here is how to read it:

- **A few settled disputes:** Normal in a large system. Not alarming on its own.
- **Repeated patterns:** Multiple suits from franchisees alleging the same thing (e.g., misrepresentation of earnings) is a red flag.
- **Recent terminations or legal actions against franchisees:** Understand why. Was it franchisee underperformance or heavy-handed enforcement?
- **Criminal matters involving officers:** Rare but possible. Any criminal history involving franchise leadership warrants serious reconsideration.

## FAQ: Reading the FDD

**Do I have to read all 200+ pages of the FDD?**
You should read it entirely, but prioritize Items 3, 6, 7, 12, 19, 20, and 21. A franchise attorney can help you focus on the highest-risk sections.

**What if a franchisor does not have an Item 19?**
Ask why. Some younger or smaller franchisors decline to include Item 19 to avoid legal liability. You can still call existing franchisees (Item 20) to gather informal performance data.

**Can the franchise agreement differ from the FDD?**
Yes. The FDD discloses terms; the franchise agreement is what you sign. Always have an attorney compare the two.

**How often is the FDD updated?**
Franchisors must update their FDD annually within 120 days of the close of their fiscal year, or more frequently if material changes occur.
