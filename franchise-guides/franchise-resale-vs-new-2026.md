# Franchise Resale vs. New Franchise: Which Should You Buy in 2026?

*Published by Franchise KI | May 2026 | 12 min read*

## Two Ways to Enter the Same System

When most people think about buying a franchise, they imagine signing a new agreement with the franchisor and building from scratch. But there's a second path that's growing rapidly in 2026: buying an existing franchise location from a current franchisee.

This is called a franchise resale. And depending on your goals, timeline, and capital, it may be significantly better—or worse—than starting fresh.

This guide gives you a complete comparison so you can make the right choice.

---

## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale is when an existing franchisee sells their location(s) to a new buyer. The brand and franchisor remain the same—you're buying the existing business, the customer base, the equipment, and the location. The franchise agreement is typically transferred and reissued.

**Why do franchisees sell?**
- Retirement (the most common reason in 2026 as Baby Boomer franchisees age out)
- Health issues
- Life change (relocation, divorce, etc.)
- Underperformance (the most concerning reason for buyers)
- Multi-unit operators streamlining their portfolio

Understanding WHY the current owner is selling is the single most important question you'll ask in a resale transaction.

## Head-to-Head Comparison

| Factor | Franchise Resale | New Franchise |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue Day 1** | Immediate (existing customers) | Zero (must build) |
| **Break-Even Timeline** | 6–18 months (from existing cash flow) | 18–36 months |
| **Purchase Price** | 2–4x EBITDA (existing business value) | Franchise fee + build-out costs |
| **Financing** | Business acquisition loans (SBA 7(a) or 504) | SBA startup franchise loans |
| **Risk of Unknown** | Existing business has history (good and bad) | Greenfield risk (no baseline) |
| **Territory** | Fixed (existing location) | May choose territory |
| **Training Requirements** | Typically same as new franchisee | Standard franchisor training |
| **Franchisor Approval** | Required for transfer | Required as new franchisee |
| **Lease** | Assumes existing lease (may be favorable or not) | New negotiation |

## The Case for Buying a Resale

### Advantage 1: Revenue From Day One

The most compelling advantage of a resale: you start generating revenue immediately. There's an existing customer base, trained staff, and operational history. You're not spending 12–18 months building from zero.

**Financial impact:** A resale generating $600,000 in annual revenue with 15% EBITDA margin produces $90,000/year in operating income. That covers your investment capital (and then some) while a new location is still burning through pre-opening marketing dollars.

### Advantage 2: Lower Risk Through Proven Performance

A resale comes with a track record. You can review:
- 3 years of profit and loss statements
- Historical sales trends (growing, flat, or declining?)
- Customer reviews and ratings
- Employee retention history
- Lease terms and remaining lease duration

With a new franchise, you're projecting performance. With a resale, you have data.

### Advantage 3: Faster Profitability

Industry data from the International Franchise Association shows resale locations typically reach profitability 12–18 months faster than new locations. For buyers who need cash flow faster (career transitions, family financial needs), this timing difference matters enormously.

### Advantage 4: Training Wheels Are Off

A new location requires you to learn everything simultaneously: operations, HR, marketing, supplier relationships, and the local market. A resale comes with trained staff and operational rhythms already established. Your learning curve focuses on the business itself, not building it from scratch.

## The Case for a New Franchise

### Advantage 1: You Choose Your Territory

Resales are geographically fixed. If you want to be in a specific suburb or city, and no resale is available there, you have no choice but to start fresh.

For home services, territory demographics matter enormously. For food, location is everything. Starting new lets you find the right site rather than accepting an existing one.

### Advantage 2: No Hidden Liabilities

When you buy a business, you risk inheriting problems the seller didn't disclose:
- Deferred maintenance on equipment
- Disgruntled employees (high turnover risk post-sale)
- Damaged community reputation
- Lease terms with personal guarantees you must assume
- Vendor relationships damaged by the previous owner

Starting new eliminates this risk entirely.

### Advantage 3: Brand-New Equipment and Build-Out

Resale locations often have aging equipment, outdated interior design, and deferred renovation costs. A new location means new equipment under warranty, a modern build-out meeting current brand standards, and zero deferred maintenance.

### Advantage 4: You Build the Culture

The culture a business has is set by its founding operator. When you buy a resale, you're inheriting someone else's culture—for better or worse. Starting fresh means you set the standards from Day 1.

## How to Evaluate a Franchise Resale: 10-Step Process

**Step 1: Request 3 years of P&L statements**
Non-negotiable. If the seller won't provide audited (or at least reviewed) financials, walk away.

**Step 2: Verify revenue through tax returns**
P&L statements can be manipulated. Tax returns cannot. Ask for 3 years of business tax returns. Any significant discrepancy between reported P&L and tax returns requires explanation.

**Step 3: Understand why they're selling**
Retirement and health are legitimate. "Too many locations" is legitimate. "The business isn't growing" requires much deeper due diligence. "Franchisor is difficult to work with" is a major red flag that affects every franchisee, including you.

**Step 4: Review the lease**
- How many years remain?
- What's the base rent? Has it been increasing?
- Are there CAM (common area maintenance) charges?
- Is there a personal guarantee you must assume?
- Can you extend if needed?

**Step 5: Evaluate the franchisor's perspective**
The franchisor must approve the transfer. Call the franchisor's franchise development team. Ask: "What's your assessment of this location?" If they're unenthusiastic, that tells you something.

**Step 6: Meet with employees**
Before closing, spend time in the location. Do the employees seem engaged? Is turnover high? Key employees who leave post-sale can devastate a resale acquisition.

**Step 7: Review customer reviews**
Check Google, Yelp, and any platform-specific reviews. A 3.2-star location in a competitive market has a brand problem, not just an operational one.

**Step 8: Verify equipment age and condition**
Get a professional equipment inspection. Replacing a walk-in cooler, HVAC system, or POS system can cost $15,000–$50,000—costs that dramatically change your acquisition math.

**Step 9: Model the acquisition price**
A fair price for a franchise resale is typically 2–4x EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortization). At 2.5x EBITDA, a business generating $90,000/year in EBITDA should sell for approximately $225,000. Add any real property (rare in franchise resales) and adjust for growth trajectory.

**Step 10: Negotiate a transition period**
Request 2–4 weeks of daily support from the seller after closing. This knowledge transfer period is valuable for operational continuity and employee retention.

## 2026 Resale Market: What's Different

**Baby Boomer retirement wave:** The largest cohort of franchise operators in history—Baby Boomers who bought franchises in the 1990s and 2000s—are reaching retirement age simultaneously. This is creating the largest franchise resale market ever. In 2026, the IFA estimates that 25–30% of all franchise transactions are resales.

**Higher valuations:** Because of the shortage of good resale inventory, prices have moved up. Expect 2.5–4x EBITDA rather than the historical 2–3x range.

**Financing evolution:** SBA 504 loans are increasingly used for resale acquisitions, particularly when real estate is involved. Bank lenders have developed specific underwriting expertise for franchise resales.

## The Bottom Line

**Buy a resale if:**
- You need cash flow faster than a new location provides
- A specific location fits your target geography
- The business has clean financials, strong reviews, and a legitimate reason for sale
- You're comfortable inheriting existing operations and culture

**Start new if:**
- Territory selection is critical to your strategy
- You want a brand-new build-out with no deferred maintenance
- You want to set culture from Day 1
- The resale market in your target brand has limited or overpriced inventory

Both paths lead to the same destination: owning a proven business system. The route depends on your capital, timeline, and risk tolerance.
