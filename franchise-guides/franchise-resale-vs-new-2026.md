## Introduction: Two Very Different Paths to Franchise Ownership

The decision to buy a franchise in 2026 is rarely simple — but one fork in the road stands out above all others: do you buy a **brand-new unit** from the franchisor, or do you acquire an **existing franchise resale** from a current owner?

Both routes lead to franchise ownership. But the risks, timelines, capital requirements, and day-one realities are fundamentally different. ## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale occurs when an existing franchisee sells their operating unit to a new owner. The buyer acquires:

- The physical location (leasehold improvements, equipment, signage)
- Existing customer base and brand recognition in that market
- Staff (often, though not always, the team stays)
- Operating history — revenue records, P&L statements, customer data
- The remaining franchise agreement term (subject to franchisor transfer approval)

The buyer does **not** necessarily get a fresh franchise term unless the franchisor grants one as part of the transfer — this is a critical negotiation point.

A new unit, by contrast, means starting from scratch: selecting a territory, signing a new franchise agreement (typically 10 years), building out or leasing a new location, hiring all staff, and generating a customer base from zero.

## Pros and Cons: Franchise Resale vs. New Unit

| Factor | Franchise Resale | New Unit |
|---|---|---|
| **Revenue on Day 1** | Yes — existing customers | No — ramp-up period (6–18 months typical) |
| **Purchase Price** | Higher upfront (includes goodwill) | Lower initial franchise fee, but build-out costs |
| **Territory Control** | Fixed — what was there before | You negotiate your territory |
| **Staff** | Inherited — good or bad | You hire fresh |
| **Brand Reputation in Market** | Already established | You build it |
| **Financial Transparency** | Real P&L available for review | Projections only (FDD Item 19 disclosures) |
| **Hidden Problems** | Risk of acquiring existing issues | Risk of underestimating ramp costs |
| **Franchisor Support** | Transfer process — limited onboarding | Full new franchisee onboarding program |
| **Lease Terms** | Inherit existing lease (terms may be unfavorable) | Negotiate fresh lease |
| **Financing** | SBA loans readily available for proven revenue | SBA loans possible but harder without revenue history |

## Price Ranges: What to Expect in 2026

Franchise resale pricing is driven by a multiple of **Seller's Discretionary Earnings (SDE)** — typically 1.5x to 3.5x SDE for franchise resales, depending on brand strength, location, and remaining lease/agreement term.

### IronCreek Barbershop (IC) — Resale Range
- **New unit investment:** $180,000 – $320,000 (franchise fee + build-out)
- **Resale range:** $150,000 – $450,000 depending on revenue and market
- **Why resales vary:** A low-performing IC location may sell below build-out cost. A high-performing unit in a prime market (e.g., Scottsdale, Austin) may command a premium.

### Silly's Hideout (SH) — Resale Range
- **New unit investment:** $250,000 – $500,000+
- **Resale range:** $200,000 – $600,000+
- **Key driver:** Children's entertainment concepts hold value well in growing suburban markets; under-managed locations can be acquired below replacement cost.

### General Franchise Resale Price Ranges (2026)

## Risk Assessment: Where Each Path Fails

### Where Resales Go Wrong
1. **Undisclosed liabilities** — back rent, vendor disputes, staffing issues the seller omits from disclosure
2. **Inherited lease problems** — above-market rent, short term remaining, unfavorable clauses
3. **Culture/reputation damage** — a location with poor reviews or staff problems takes time to rehabilitate
4. **Franchisor approval delays** — transfer approval can take 60–90 days; deals fall apart
5. **Overpaying for goodwill** — a buyer emotionally attached to a specific location may pay above market

### Where New Units Go Wrong
1. **Ramp underestimation** — expecting profitability in month 3, experiencing losses through month 14
2. **Territory mistakes** — selecting demographics that do not match the brand's ideal customer
3. **Build-out overruns** — construction costs in 2026 remain elevated; budget overruns of 20–30% are common
4. **Working capital shortfall** — most new franchisees underfund their operating reserve
5. **Competitive saturation** — entering a market where adjacent operators are already established

## Due Diligence Checklist for Franchise Resales

Before making an offer on any franchise resale, verify:

- [ ] Three full years of tax returns (not just P&L — tax returns cannot be easily manipulated)
- [ ] Current lease copy with all amendments and remaining term
- [ ] Franchise agreement copy + transfer fee amount
- [ ] Franchisor approval requirements and timeline
- [ ] All equipment condition + age + lease/owned status
- [ ] Staffing agreements — who stays, who leaves, at what wage
- [ ] Any pending litigation or franchisor violations
- [ ] Customer reviews and reputation audit (Google, Yelp, brand-specific)
- [ ] Local competitive landscape — new entrants, market shifts
- [ ] Reason for sale — validate with franchisor, not just seller

### IronCreek Barbershop — Resale Scenario
An IronCreek location in a suburban Texas market with $420K annual revenue, $95K SDE, and 4 years remaining on lease might list for $190,000–$240,000 (2.0–2.5x SDE). **New unit verdict for SH:** Prefer new units in high-growth suburban corridors. Resales make sense when the location is underperforming and you can diagnose a fixable problem (management, marketing, staffing).

## The 2026 Financing Landscape

Both paths have financing options, but the resale route often has an advantage:

**SBA 7(a) loans** — available for both, but lenders prefer resales with documented revenue. A resale with 3 years of tax returns showing profitability is a dramatically easier loan conversation than a projection-based new unit request.

**Seller financing** — common in resales; sellers will often carry 10–30% of the purchase price to bridge a deal. This is not available in new unit purchases.

**Franchisor financing programs** — some franchisors offer in-house financing or preferred lender relationships that apply to both new and resale transactions.

**ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups)** — used by some buyers to deploy retirement funds without penalty; works for both paths but carries significant risk and requires specialist legal/tax counsel.

## Making the Decision: A Simple Framework

**Choose a resale when:**
- You want to generate income faster (within 30–90 days of close)
- You have identified a specific underperforming unit with a diagnosable, fixable problem
- You are less experienced with building teams from scratch
- Financing is easier to secure with documented revenue

**Choose a new unit when:**
- You have identified a high-growth territory with no established competition
- You want a full franchise term (10 years) with fresh lease negotiations
- You have working capital to sustain a 12–18 month ramp period
- Brand-building from scratch aligns with your entrepreneurial style
