# Franchise Resale vs. New Franchise: Which Is the Better Buy in 2026?

*Published by FranchiseKI | May 2026 | Reading time: 6 minutes*

When people think about buying a franchise, they typically imagine signing on with a franchisor and opening a brand-new location. But there's another option worth serious consideration: buying an existing franchise unit from a current franchisee.

Both paths have merit. The right choice depends on your financial situation, risk tolerance, and how quickly you want to reach profitability.

## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale is the purchase of an existing, operating franchise unit from its current owner. The business already has:
- An established customer base and revenue history
- Trained staff (typically)
- Equipment already purchased and installed
- Lease already in place
- Operating systems set up and running

You're not starting from zero. You're buying an operating business that happens to be a franchise.

## Why Franchisees Sell

Understanding why an owner is selling is critical due diligence:

**Common legitimate reasons:**
- Retirement
- Health issues
- Relocation
- Personal or family circumstances
- Portfolio diversification (multi-unit operators selling one unit to fund another)

**Warning signs to investigate:**
- Location underperforming despite years of operation
- Competitive threats in the market
- Lease issues (short remaining term, unfavorable renewal terms)
- Staffing problems in a labor-constrained market
- Franchisor conflicts

Always ask: *Why is this owner selling?* Then verify the answer independently through franchisee validation calls with other units in the system.

## Franchise Resale Advantages

**1. Immediate Cash Flow**
An established location generates revenue from day one. For buyers who need income quickly, this can be decisive.

**2. Verified Financial History**
The unit has tax returns, P&L statements, and sales data from prior years. You're not projecting from Item 19 averages—you're looking at actual performance from this specific location.

**3. Shorter Path to Profitability**
New franchise buildouts typically require 6–18 months before reaching break-even. A resale location may already be profitable.

**4. Lower Uncertainty**
You know the customer base, the local competition, and the territory dynamics before you buy.

**5. Existing Staff**
Trained employees often stay through ownership transitions, reducing the startup labor burden.

## Franchise Resale Disadvantages

**1. Higher Purchase Price**
You're paying a premium for established revenue. Resale prices often include goodwill, which can be difficult to value accurately.

**2. Inherited Problems**
Equipment wear, deferred maintenance, staff issues, or customer service damage from the prior owner.

**3. Limited Location Selection**
You're restricted to available units, not all markets.

**4. Older Equipment**
Equipment may be approaching end-of-life, requiring capital investment soon after purchase.

**5. Franchisor Transfer Fees**
Most franchisors charge a transfer fee (typically $5K–$25K) and require the new owner to complete training.

## New Franchise Advantages

**1. Greenfield Territory**
You select your location, negotiate your own lease, and build your customer base in a market of your choosing.

**2. Modern Build-Out**
New equipment, current technology, and the latest brand standards from day one.

**3. Lower Acquisition Cost**
You pay the franchise fee and buildout costs—not a premium for someone else's goodwill.

**4. Fresh Relationships**
You start with no inherited staff conflicts, no reputation baggage, and a clean slate.

## New Franchise Disadvantages

**1. Longer Path to Revenue**
Build-out and ramp-up take time. Most new franchise locations don't reach full revenue potential for 12–24 months.

**2. Higher Risk**
Location risk is real. Even with franchise support, not every market supports every concept equally well.

**3. Capital Tied Up Longer**
Build-out requires capital before any return is generated.

## The Financial Comparison

| Factor | Franchise Resale | New Franchise |
|--------|-----------------|---------------|
| Time to first revenue | Day 1 | 3–18 months (build-out) |
| Price paid | Revenue multiple + assets | Franchise fee + build-out |
| Financial history available | Yes | No (based on Item 19 estimates) |
| Location choice | Limited to available units | Your selection |
| Equipment condition | Varies (may need investment) | New |
| Ramp-up risk | Lower | Higher |
| Staff | Often inherited | Must hire |

## How to Evaluate a Franchise Resale

**Step 1: Request 3 years of financials**
P&L statements, tax returns, and point-of-sale revenue data. Any seller unwilling to provide these should be treated with skepticism.

**Step 2: Verify the reason for sale independently**
Don't just accept the seller's explanation. Call other franchisees in the system and ask whether this location is known as a strong or weak performer.

**Step 3: Get the lease reviewed**
How many years remain? What are the renewal terms? Is the rent at market? A bad lease is a deal-killer regardless of revenue.

**Step 4: Conduct a physical inspection**
Equipment age and condition. HVAC, plumbing, electrical. Deferred maintenance represents future capital requirements.

**Step 5: Understand the transfer process**
What does the franchisor require for approval? What's the transfer fee? Will the franchisor be supportive of this transition?
