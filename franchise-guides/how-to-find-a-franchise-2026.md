# How to Find a Franchise: The 2026 Complete System

*A step-by-step guide from research to signing day*

---

## Step 1: Get Clear on Your "Franchise Profile" (Week 1-2)

Before you look at a single franchise listing, answer these questions honestly:

**Capital Assessment:**
- Liquid assets available: $_____
- Total investable capital (including loans): $_____
- Monthly income needed from day one: $_____

**Lifestyle Assessment:**
- Owner-operator (full-time, hands-on) vs. semi-absentee (part-time, managers run it)
- Can you handle employees? How many?
- Geographic flexibility? (Local only, or open to another city?)
- Timeline to opening: 6 months, 12 months, 18 months?

**Skills and Background:**
- Industry experience? (Food service, retail, B2B, home services, etc.)
- Sales and marketing comfort level?
- Operations and systems orientation?

Your answers narrow 3,000+ franchise options down to a manageable shortlist of 20-50.

## Step 3: Consult a Franchise Broker (Week 3-6)

A quality franchise broker is like a buyer's agent in real estate — they guide you, help you avoid mistakes, and get paid by the seller (franchisor) when a deal closes.

**The important caveat:** Not all brokers are equal. Some prioritize the brands with highest referral fees. The best brokers use data to match you.

**Recommended:** Franchise KI (franchiseki.com) — they use MapKi's data to match buyers against verified FDD financial data.

What a good broker provides:
- Deeper research on your shortlisted brands
- Access to franchisees you can call for honest feedback
- Help understanding the FDD
- Negotiating support (some terms in the franchise agreement can be modified)

## Step 4: Research Your Top 3-5 Brands (Week 4-10)

For each brand on your shortlist:

### A. Review Item 19 Carefully

Item 19 of the FDD is the financial performance representation. Look for:
- **Average Unit Volume (AUV)** — annual gross revenue per location
- **Median net income** (less common but most useful)
- **Range** — what do the top 25% and bottom 25% of franchisees earn?
- **How many units are included** in the data set? (Sample size matters)

**Red flag:** Franchisors who don't publish any Item 19 data. Ask why. "We don't make earnings claims" is not acceptable for a $200K investment decision.

### B. Call Current Franchisees

Item 20 of the FDD lists current franchisees with contact information. Call at least 5-10, especially ones who:
- Are in markets similar to yours (size, demographics)
- Have been operating 2-4 years (past the hard first year, past the honeymoon phase)

Ask them:
- What would you do differently if you were starting over?
- What does the franchisor support actually look like vs. what was promised?
- Did the Item 19 data match your experience?
- Are you profitable? Happy?

### C. Review Items 3, 7, and 21

- **Item 3:** Litigation history — how many franchise disputes? Active lawsuits from franchisees?
- **Item 7:** Total initial investment breakdown (trust this more than verbal estimates)
- **Item 21:** Franchisor's financial statements — is the franchisor financially healthy?

### D. Attend Discovery Day

Most franchisors require an in-person "discovery day" at their headquarters before signing. This is a mutual evaluation — they're deciding if you're a good franchisee; you're deciding if you like the culture and people.

## Step 5: Territory Selection and Site Search (Week 8-14)

For brick-and-mortar franchises:
- Get your territory in writing before you commit
- Review demographic data for your territory (the franchisor should provide this)
- Site selection support varies enormously by franchise system — ask how involved they are

For home-based or mobile franchises:
- Confirm territory boundaries and exclusivity
- Understand what happens if the territory splits due to growth

---

## Step 6: Financing (Weeks 6-14, parallel)

**SBA 7(a) loans:** Most common franchise financing. Covers up to 90% of total investment for SBA-approved franchise brands. Terms: 10 years for equipment, up to 25 years with real estate.

**401(k) Rollover (ROBS):** Use retirement funds to invest in your franchise without early withdrawal penalties. Requires specific legal structure. Work with a firm specializing in this.

**Franchisor Financing:** Some franchise brands offer deferred fees or in-house financing. Less common but worth asking.

**Home Equity:** Second mortgage or HELOC. Lower interest rate but your home is collateral.

**Get pre-qualified early** — before you visit any franchise discovery day.

---

## Step 7: Legal Review and Signing (Week 12-16+)

**Hire a franchise attorney.** Not a general business attorney — a franchise specialist. This costs $1,500–$3,000 and is non-negotiable. A good attorney will:
- Flag unusual terms in the franchise agreement
- Explain what's negotiable (more than most buyers think)
- Identify areas of risk specific to your state and market

**Never sign a franchise agreement without attorney review.**
