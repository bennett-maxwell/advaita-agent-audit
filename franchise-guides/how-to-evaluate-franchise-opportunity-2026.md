# How to Evaluate a Franchise Opportunity: The 10-Step Process (2026)

*Published by Franchise KI | May 2026 | 13 min read*

## Why Most Buyers Evaluate Franchises Wrong

Most people approach franchise evaluation the way they approach buying a car: they fall in love with the brand, imagine themselves in the role, and then try to rationalize the numbers. That emotional-first approach leads to expensive mistakes.

The right process is the opposite: start with data, verify with people, and let emotion be the final confirmation—not the starting point.

Here's the 10-step evaluation process used by experienced franchise buyers and franchise consultants.

---

## Step 1: Verify You Meet the Financial Requirements

Before you invest a single hour evaluating a franchise, confirm you qualify financially.

**Minimum requirements vary, but the framework is consistent:**
- **Liquid capital:** Usually 20-30% of total investment. This is cash you can access without liquidating retirement accounts.
- **Net worth:** Most franchisors require 2-3x the liquid capital requirement in total net worth.
- **Credit score:** SBA lenders typically require 680+. Some franchisors have minimum requirements in the FDD.

**Why this comes first:** It's a waste of your time and the franchisor's time to spend 60 days in discovery only to be declined for financing. Know your financial position before you start.

---

## Step 2: Request and Read the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document)

The FDD is a legal document the franchisor must provide at least 14 days before you sign anything or pay any fees. It's 23 items covering everything about the franchise relationship.

**Critical items to prioritize:**
- **Item 3:** Litigation history (lawsuits against the franchisor—pattern of litigation is a red flag)
- **Item 6:** Fees (all royalties, marketing fees, transfer fees, renewal fees)
- **Item 7:** Initial investment range (the full cost picture)
- **Item 12:** Territory provisions (how protected are you? What can the franchisor do in your territory?)
- **Item 19:** Financial performance representations (the actual revenue data)
- **Item 20:** Current and former franchisee list (your validation source)
- **Item 21:** Franchisor financial statements (audited—is the franchisor financially healthy?)

Most buyers skim the FDD. Buyers who read it carefully avoid 80% of regrettable franchise decisions.

---

## Step 3: Analyze Item 19 Carefully

Item 19 is voluntary—not all franchisors provide it. Those that don't should face extra scrutiny.

**What Item 19 can reveal:**
- Average unit volume (AUV) for franchised locations
- Range of performance (top quartile vs. bottom quartile)
- How many locations are included in the data (system-wide vs. cherry-picked)
- Whether data is for franchised units only, or includes corporate units (which may outperform)

**Critical question:** "Is this data for all franchised locations, or only those open for 2+ years?"

New locations typically underperform because they're building a customer base. If the data excludes locations under 2 years old, the AUV will be inflated vs. what you'll experience in Year 1.

**What if there's no Item 19?**
Ask the franchisor for any information they can legally share. Also call existing franchisees directly—they can tell you their revenue if they choose to.

---

## Step 4: Model the Full Financial Projection

Using Item 7 (investment range) and Item 19 (revenue data), build your financial model:

**The 5-number model:**
1. **Total investment** (use Item 7 midpoint, not the low end)
2. **Projected annual revenue** (use median AUV from Item 19, not top quartile)
3. **Projected gross margin** (industry benchmarks + franchisor guidance)
4. **Operating expenses** (rent, labor, supplies, royalties, marketing fees)
5. **EBITDA** (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization)

**Break-even calculation:**
Total investment ÷ Annual EBITDA = Years to break-even

Target: Under 3 years. Over 5 years = proceed with extreme caution.

**Royalty impact calculation:**
For every $100K in revenue, you'll pay $4,000–$10,000 in royalties annually.
Over a 10-year franchise agreement, that's $40,000–$100,000 per $100K of revenue.

---

## Step 5: Validate with Existing Franchisees

This is the most valuable step in the entire process—and the most underutilized.

**How many to call:** At least 15–20. Call all sizes: single-unit, multi-unit, new locations, mature locations, your target geography.

**Questions to ask:**
- "What's your annual revenue? Is it in line with what you projected?"
- "What does corporate support actually look like week-to-week?"
- "What's the hardest part of this business that nobody told you about?"
- "If you could go back and change one thing, what would it be?"
- "Would you buy this franchise again?"
- "Would you buy more units?"

**The most important question:** "Why wouldn't you buy this franchise again?" If they say "I would absolutely buy it again"—ask what gives them pause. Everyone has something.

**Green flags in validation:**
- 80%+ of franchisees would "definitely" buy again
- Active owner community (they're helping each other succeed)
- Franchisees opening additional units (multi-unit expansion = confidence signal)

**Red flags:**
- Franchisees reluctant to speak (fear of franchisor retaliation is a pattern)
- High variance in results (some killing it, many struggling—system problems often lurk here)
- Significant complaints about corporate support not matching the pitch

---

## Step 6: Hire a Franchise Attorney

Non-negotiable. A franchise agreement is a 10–20-year legal commitment worth hundreds of thousands of dollars. Spend $2,000–$4,000 on a specialized franchise attorney.

**What your attorney reviews:**
- Territorial protections (what can't the franchisor do in your territory?)
- Transfer rights (can you sell, and at what terms?)
- Renewal terms (what's required for renewal and at what additional cost?)
- Termination provisions (what can get you terminated? Is the process fair?)
- Dispute resolution (mandatory arbitration? Where? Whose law governs?)

**Common negotiation wins your attorney can achieve:**
- Reduced personal guarantee period (3–5 years vs. full term)
- Right of first refusal on adjacent territories
- Clarified encroachment provisions for online ordering and delivery

---

## Step 7: Evaluate the Territory

Territory is one of the most frequently misunderstood aspects of franchise agreements.

**Questions to investigate:**
- What are the exact boundaries of my territory?
- What activities is the franchisor restricted from within my territory?
- Can the franchisor sell to my territory via online/delivery channels?
- What happens to my territory if I underperform?
- Are there already other franchisees near my territory who might affect my customer base?

**Population density benchmarks:**
Most franchise brands have minimum population requirements per territory. If your territory is at the minimum threshold, your economics will be at the lower end of Item 19 performance data.

---

## Step 8: Do a Site Visit (For Brick-and-Mortar)

If the franchise requires a physical location, visit at least 3 existing franchise locations—ideally without announcing yourself.

**What to evaluate:**
- Customer traffic (observe multiple times: morning rush, lunch, dinner)
- Staff engagement and training quality
- Cleanliness and operational standards
- Customer service quality
- How long the wait times are (too long = understaffed; no wait = too slow)

**Red flag:** Locations that look tired, understaffed, or below brand standards indicate a systemwide enforcement problem.

---

## Step 9: Attend Discovery Day (and Prepare for It)

Discovery Day is when the franchisor invites serious candidates to headquarters for a 1–2 day immersion experience. Come prepared.

**Questions to ask the executive team at Discovery Day:**
- "What's your plan for the next 3–5 years?"
- "What percentage of your franchisees are multi-unit operators?"
- "What's your support ratio? How many franchisees does each support rep handle?"
- "What's your technology roadmap?"
- "What's your process for handling franchisees who are struggling?"

**What to observe:**
- Is the team cohesive and knowledgeable?
- Do they answer hard questions directly or deflect?
- What's the culture of the corporate team?

---

## Step 10: Make the Decision With the Full Picture

After completing Steps 1–9, you have everything you need to make a data-driven decision.

**A franchise is the right choice when:**
- Item 19 shows consistent performance with limited downside variance
- 80%+ of franchisees would buy again
- Your financial model shows break-even under 3 years
- Territory is strong and protections are clear
- The franchisor team is cohesive, experienced, and well-funded
- Your attorney found no material legal concerns

**Walk away when:**
- Item 19 shows high variance (some winning, many struggling)
- Franchisees are reluctant to validate or reveal systemic concerns
- Financial model shows break-even over 5 years
- Territory protections are weak or ambiguous
- Franchisor has significant litigation history in Item 3
