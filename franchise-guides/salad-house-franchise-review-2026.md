# Salad House Franchise Review 2026: Costs, Performance, and What Buyers Need to Know

Salad House has become one of the more recognizable names in fast casual health food franchising on the East Coast. Founded in New Jersey and franchising since 2017, the brand has built a loyal regional following on customizable salads, grain bowls, and wraps — and has been quietly expanding its franchise footprint for the better part of a decade.

If you're evaluating Salad House as a franchise investment in 2026, this review covers what you actually need to know: real investment numbers, what the financial performance data shows, who this opportunity is best suited for, and how the discovery process works.

## Brand Overview

Salad House was founded in 2011 in Summit, New Jersey, by Joey Ciaglia. The brand's positioning — fresh, customizable, made-to-order — put it ahead of the fast casual health trend before that category exploded. It began franchising in 2017 and has since expanded primarily throughout the Northeast, with locations in New Jersey, New York, Connecticut, and select markets further south.

The menu centers on build-your-own salads with a range of protein options, housemade dressings, and grain bowl bases. It's positioned as an everyday dining option — not a premium health food destination — with price points that compete directly with Sweetgreen and similar concepts.

As of 2026, Salad House operates a mix of company-owned and franchise locations, with the majority of growth coming from franchise development.

## Investment Range: $350K–$500K Total

Salad House is not a budget franchise. Total investment for a new unit runs approximately **$350,000 to $500,000**, depending on real estate, market, and build conditions. Here's a general breakdown of where the money goes:

- **Franchise fee:** Approximately $40,000–$50,000
- **Leasehold improvements / build-out:** $150,000–$250,000 (highly variable by market)
- **Equipment and fixtures:** $80,000–$130,000
- **Initial inventory and supplies:** $10,000–$20,000
- **Working capital:** $50,000–$75,000 (minimum recommended)
- **Training, pre-opening, and miscellaneous:** $20,000–$40,000

Buyers should treat the high end of the range as the planning baseline, particularly in high-cost markets like metro New York or New England where real estate and contractor costs routinely exceed franchise estimates.

SBA financing is available for qualified buyers. With $150,000+ in liquid capital, most buyers can structure a deal that doesn't require full self-funding.

## Item 19: What the Financial Performance Data Shows

Salad House publishes financial performance data in Item 19 of its Franchise Disclosure Document. While specific figures update annually, the brand has disclosed average unit volume (AUV) data that franchise buyers can use to build projections.

Key context for reading any Item 19:
- **Averages include outliers.** A small number of high-performing corporate or top-franchise units can inflate the mean. Ask specifically for median figures and for performance data broken out by unit age (Year 1 vs. Year 3+).
- **Gross revenue is not profit.** Item 19 often leads with top-line sales figures. You need to model your own costs — rent, labor (typically 28–35% of revenue in fast casual), food cost (target sub-30%), royalties, and marketing fees — to estimate actual net.
- **Ramp time is real.** Most Salad House franchisees report that months 1–6 are below the longer-run average as the location builds its customer base.

Prospective buyers should request a copy of the most current FDD directly from Salad House and ideally have a franchise attorney or experienced broker review Item 19 line by line.

## The Ideal Salad House Buyer

Based on placement experience and franchisee profiles, the Salad House buyer who succeeds tends to share several characteristics:

**Investor mindset, not chef mindset.** Salad House operators who struggle are often those who got into it because they love food. The ones who thrive treat it as a business investment: they hire and retain good managers, monitor labor percentages weekly, and run it like a P&L-driven enterprise.

**$150,000+ in liquid capital.** Given the investment range and the importance of adequate working capital, buyers with less than $150K liquid are at meaningful risk of undercapitalization. The brand may qualify buyers with less, but under-funded launches are a leading predictor of struggle.

**Operational experience or strong local management networks.** Salad House is not designed to be semi-absentee in the traditional sense. Most successful operators are meaningfully involved in operations, at least in the first two years.

**Market fit awareness.** Salad House performs best in markets with a health-conscious consumer base and strong lunch traffic (office parks, medical districts, college corridors, dense suburban retail). Rural markets or price-sensitive demographics have produced mixed results.

## The Discovery Process: What to Expect

For most franchise buyers, the Salad House discovery process runs 45–90 days from initial contact to signing. Here's the general arc:

1. **Initial inquiry and intro call** — Brand development team covers the high-level overview
2. **FDD delivery and review period** — You have at least 14 calendar days by law before signing anything
3. **Franchise attorney review** — Non-optional for serious buyers
4. **Franchisee validation calls** — You call existing operators; ask for a full list, not a cherry-picked one
5. **Discovery Day** — On-site visit to New Jersey headquarters; meeting with leadership team
6. **Territory award and signing** — Franchise agreement executed; initial fee paid

The FDD review and validation calls are the most information-dense parts of the process. Buyers who rush these steps to close faster tend to have more regrets post-signing.

## Competitive Context

Salad House competes in a crowded fast casual health segment that includes Sweetgreen (public company, significant brand equity), Tender Loving Tacos, local independents, and a growing set of newer franchise concepts. Its regional strength in the Northeast is a genuine asset. Its relative lack of national brand awareness compared to Sweetgreen is a real consideration for buyers in new markets.

For buyers specifically in the Mid-Atlantic and Northeast, Salad House's existing brand pull reduces the marketing investment needed to generate opening traffic. For buyers opening in markets where the name is unknown, budget accordingly for local awareness campaigns.

## Who Should Skip Salad House (and Look Elsewhere)

- Buyers with less than $150K liquid who can't access financing
- Anyone looking for a passive or semi-absentee model
- Buyers in states or markets where health-casual dining hasn't yet hit critical mass
- First-time business owners without prior management or operations experience (not a hard rule, but a meaningful risk factor)
