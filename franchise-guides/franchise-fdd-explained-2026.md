# Franchise Disclosure Document Explained: What Every Buyer Needs to Know in 2026

The Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) is the most important document you'll review before investing in a franchise. It's not marketing material—it's a legally required disclosure of material facts about the franchisor, the franchise system, and the financial realities of franchise ownership. Understanding the FDD's 23 items is the foundation of informed franchise decision-making. This guide breaks down each item and highlights what separates promising opportunities from risky ones.

## What Is a Franchise Disclosure Document?

A Franchise Disclosure Document is a comprehensive written disclosure that franchisors are legally required to provide to prospective franchisees at least 14 days before they sign a franchise agreement or pay any fees. The FDD requirement is enforced under the Federal Trade Commission's Franchise Rule and state franchise laws.

The purpose of the FDD is simple: provide franchisees with enough information to make an informed investment decision. Unlike marketing materials, the FDD contains audited financial statements, litigation history, officer backgrounds, and detailed representations about franchise profitability (when available).

### Why the FDD Matters

The FDD is your transparency window into the franchisor's financial health, legal history, and the real economics of running their franchise. Franchisors cannot legally misrepresent, omit, or downplay material facts in the FDD. Violations can result in FTC enforcement, state actions, and private lawsuits. This legal obligation makes the FDD far more reliable than a franchisor's sales presentation.

Many franchisees report that reviewing the FDD thoroughly would have revealed red flags they missed during the sales process. The FDD doesn't guarantee success, but it dramatically reduces the risk of discovering unpleasant surprises after you've invested your capital.

## The 23 FDD Items: Complete Breakdown

### Item 1: Franchisor and Predecessors

Item 1 identifies the franchisor's legal business name, business form (LLC, Corporation, etc.), and any predecessor companies. This establishes the franchisor's identity and any history under different corporate structures.

**What to look for**: If the franchisor has changed names or restructured multiple times in a short period, that's a yellow flag. Serial rebranding can indicate prior failures, regulatory issues, or acquisition by new ownership that fundamentally changed the business.

### Item 2: Business Experience of Officers and Directors

Item 2 lists every officer and director of the franchisor company, including their title, educational background, and business experience. This section typically covers the previous five years of employment.

**What to look for**: Do the leadership team have relevant industry experience? Is there franchise system experience? Turnover in key positions (CEO, COO, VP of Franchise Development) within the last 1-2 years might indicate internal problems or destabilization.

### Item 3: Business Experience of Franchise Broker

If the franchisor uses a franchise broker or consultant to sell franchises, Item 3 discloses that broker's background, compensation, and whether they have disclosed the broker relationship in sales materials.

**What to look for**: Some franchisors rely heavily on brokers who are incentivized by sales commissions rather than franchisee success. This creates potential conflicts of interest.

### Item 4: Previous Franchise Experience

Item 4 discloses whether the franchisor or its officers have previously offered franchises in other systems. This establishes whether the current team has experience managing franchise networks.

**What to look for**: If key officers have no prior franchise experience, be cautious. Running a franchise system is materially different from running a single location or a non-franchised chain.

### Item 5: Public Figures and Endorsements

Item 5 lists any celebrities, public figures, or athletes who endorse the franchise system and their compensation for that endorsement. This ensures transparency about paid endorsements that might appear organic in marketing.

**What to look for**: Ensure endorsements are recent and the endorser is actually affiliated with the brand. Outdated endorsements or figures no longer associated with the brand suggest franchise marketing challenges.

### Item 6: Financial Information

Item 6 provides audited financial statements of the franchisor, typically the last two years of balance sheets and income statements. This is critical for assessing franchisor financial stability.

**What to look for**: Is the franchisor profitable? Are revenues growing? Are receivables (money owed by franchisees) climbing? Declining revenue or growing receivables can signal franchisee struggles.

### Item 7: Litigation History

Item 7 lists all material litigation involving the franchisor, its officers, and predecessors during the past 10 years, excluding minor small claims. This includes lawsuits, arbitrations, and regulatory actions.

**What to look for**: Litigation is normal in franchise systems, but patterns matter. Multiple lawsuits from franchisees alleging misrepresentation, multiple actions by regulators, or litigation with customers suggest systemic problems. Pay attention to claims about false earnings representations or undisclosed costs.

### Item 8: Bankruptcy History

Item 8 discloses any bankruptcy filings by the franchisor, predecessors, or key officers during the past 10 years. This reveals financial distress history.

**What to look for**: A bankruptcy in the franchisor's past isn't automatically disqualifying, but it indicates the company faced severe financial challenges. If the franchisor is now recovered and thriving, it might be fine. If recent, it's a significant concern.

### Item 9: Initial and Ongoing Fees

Item 9 clearly lists all fees you'll pay, including the initial franchise fee, training fees, equipment costs, technology fees, royalties, marketing fees, and any other recurring charges. This is where hidden costs often hide.

**What to look for**: Compare the total franchisee investment across Item 9 and Item 7 (estimated startup costs) against Item 19 financial performance data. Do franchisees generate enough revenue to cover these fees and earn a profit?

### Item 10: Initial Investment

Item 10 provides a detailed breakdown of the estimated initial investment required to start the franchise, organized by category (franchise fee, real estate, equipment, working capital, etc.). This is typically presented as a range.

**What to look for**: Is this estimate realistic based on your market? Contact existing franchisees and ask if their actual costs matched these estimates. Underestimating initial investment is a common franchisee trap.

### Item 11: Assistance and Advertising

Item 11 describes the support the franchisor provides, including pre-opening training, ongoing support, field visits, marketing materials, national advertising campaigns, and technology systems. This establishes the franchisor's support obligations.

**What to look for**: How extensive is the training? Is it adequate for someone new to the industry? Does the franchisor provide ongoing support or do you fend for yourself after opening? Are marketing materials proprietary or generic?

### Item 12: Territory and Exclusivity

Item 12 explains whether franchisees receive territorial exclusivity, how territory is defined, and whether the franchisor or other franchisees can operate competing locations nearby.

**What to look for**: If there's no exclusivity, the franchisor could open a competing unit next door. Even with exclusivity, verify the territory definition is geographically reasonable (not a 20-block radius that overlaps with three other franchisees).

### Item 13: Trademarks and Intellectual Property

Item 13 lists all federally registered trademarks, service marks, and trade names the franchisee will use. It also discloses any pending trademark applications or challenges.

**What to look for**: Are trademarks owned by the franchisor or licensed? If licensed, could the license be terminated? Unregistered trademarks offer less legal protection. Trademark disputes can threaten your entire business.

### Item 14: Patents and Intellectual Property

Item 14 discloses any patents, copyrights, trade secrets, or proprietary systems the franchisor claims. It also notes whether these are registered and any pending litigation over IP rights.

**What to look for**: If the franchise is built on proprietary technology or systems, verify they're actually protected and not easily replicated by competitors.

### Item 15: Participation in Operation

Item 15 explains whether the franchisor requires owner-operator involvement or permits passive ownership. It also discloses any requirements for you to perform specific duties.

**What to look for**: Some franchises require owner presence; others are designed for passive investors using hired management. Clarify expectations before signing.

### Item 16: Restrictions on Products and Services

Item 16 lists any restrictions on what products or services you can sell, what suppliers you must use, and any rebates or other compensation the franchisor receives from suppliers.

**What to look for**: Mandatory supplier relationships can inflate your costs. Some franchisors receive kickbacks from suppliers—these should be disclosed. If you're restricted from offering products franchisees want, revenue suffers.

### Item 17: Renewal and Termination

Item 17 describes the franchise agreement term, renewal options, termination rights, and post-termination obligations. This is critical because it defines the conditions under which you can be terminated.

**What to look for**: How long is the initial term (typically 5-10 years)? Do you have automatic renewal rights? What triggers termination? Can the franchisor terminate for convenience or only for cause? What happens to inventory and signage when the franchise ends?

### Item 18: Public Figures and Celebrities

Item 18 is a duplicate/related to Item 5, providing additional detail on any public figure endorsements.

**What to look for**: Same as Item 5—verify endorsements are current and legitimate.

### Item 19: Financial Performance Representations

Item 19 is arguably the most important item. It contains financial performance data, system-wide sales averages, unit volumes, and profitability information. Not all franchisors provide Item 19, but those who do offer crucial benchmarking data.

**What to look for**: This is where you determine whether franchises actually make money. Key metrics to evaluate:

- **Average Unit Volumes (AUV)**: Median and average revenue per location. If the AUV is $400,000 but your operating costs are $350,000, margins are thin.
- **Percentage of franchisees reaching breakeven**: Item 19 often shows what percentage of franchisees achieved profitability. If only 30% are profitable, that's a major red flag.
- **Earnings before owner draw**: Does Item 19 show franchisee earnings after franchise fees, or before? This dramatically changes the numbers.
- **Franchise tenure**: Franchisees in system for 3+ years usually perform better than those in first year. Use Item 19 data to compare new franchisees to mature ones.

For context, Indy Clover's Item 19 demonstrates the booth-rental resale model economics, showing revenue potential across different franchise tenures. Salad House's Item 19 provides benchmark fast-casual metrics for the health-focused segment.

If the franchisor doesn't provide Item 19, ask why. Legitimate franchisors willing to stand behind their numbers provide this data. Refusal to provide financial performance representations is a major warning sign.

### Item 20: Financial Obligations

Item 20 lists all financial obligations franchisees assume beyond royalties and fees mentioned in Item 9. This includes lease obligations, equipment purchases, insurance, equipment maintenance, and any other significant financial commitments.

**What to look for**: Are there hidden financial obligations? Does the franchisor require you to carry specific insurance types (general liability, workers' comp) that cost more than necessary? Are you obligated to lease equipment from the franchisor at marked-up prices?

### Item 21: Relationship with Franchisor and Franchisee Associates

Item 21 lists any financial relationships between the franchisor and its franchisees, beyond the standard franchise agreement. This includes if the franchisor sells products to franchisees, leases real estate, provides financing, or operates company-owned locations in the same territory.

**What to look for**: Does the franchisor operate competing units? If so, it might prioritize its own units over franchisees. Does the franchisor require you to buy products at inflated prices? These relationships can create conflicts of interest.

### Item 22: Renewal, Termination, and Transfer

Item 22 provides a detailed table showing, for each franchisor location, whether the franchise was renewed, terminated, transferred, or otherwise concluded during the past year. This is the "franchisee outcomes" data.

**What to look for**: What percentage of franchises are renewed versus terminated? High termination rates suggest franchisee dissatisfaction. High transfer rates suggest franchisees want out. Look for patterns: Are newer franchisees terminating at higher rates? Are certain regions performing worse?

### Item 23: Representative Contacts

Item 23 provides contact information for current and former franchisees, so you can call them directly and ask about their experience. This is your direct line to the truth.

**What to look for**: Contact multiple franchisees (at least 5-10), including recent franchisees and veterans. Ask about profitability, franchisor support, and whether they'd do it again. Don't rely on the franchisor's "approved" franchisee list—ask for random samples and franchisees who may have left the system.

## Critical Red Flags in the FDD

**Missing Item 19**: If the franchisor doesn't provide Item 19 financial performance representations, question why. Ask directly. A refusal or vague answer is a red flag.

**Undefined costs**: If Item 10 shows "estimated" investment with wide ranges or missing categories, dig deeper. Real franchisees can tell you actual costs.

**Litigation patterns**: Multiple lawsuits alleging misrepresentation or undisclosed costs suggest systemic problems.

**High termination rates in Item 22**: If more than 10-15% of franchises terminate annually, something is wrong.

**Supplier relationships without disclosure**: If you discover the franchisor receives kickbacks from suppliers or mandates inefficient suppliers, that's a breach of trust.

**Non-compete clauses that persist post-termination**: Some franchisors prevent you from competing in your industry for years after termination, effectively trapping you in an unprofitable franchise.

## How to Review the FDD Effectively

**Get legal counsel**: Have a franchise attorney review the FDD before you sign anything. The $1,500-$3,000 investment in legal review is trivial compared to a bad franchise investment.

**Create a comparison spreadsheet**: If you're evaluating multiple franchises, build a spreadsheet with key metrics (franchise fee, royalty, Item 19 AUV, startup costs, termination rate, litigation history).

**Contact franchisees from Item 23**: This is non-negotiable. Call at least 10 franchisees, including some not provided by the franchisor. Ask about profitability, regrets, and whether they'd recommend the system.

**Request Item 19 data in detail**: If Item 19 exists, request additional breakdowns. What percentage of franchisees are profitable? What's the median AUV versus average (median is more useful)? How do regional variations affect profitability?

**Compare against Item 9 and 10**: Cross-reference franchise fees, ongoing royalties, and startup costs against Item 19 profitability. If fees exceed typical franchisee earnings, the math doesn't work.

## FAQ: Understanding the Franchise Disclosure Document

**Q: Is the FDD required for all franchises?**

A: The FDD is required by federal law (FTC Franchise Rule) for all franchise offerings, with limited exceptions. Some small franchises or certain business models might have exemptions, but if you're buying a franchise from a franchisor, you should receive an FDD.

**Q: How long should I take to review the FDD?**

A: At minimum 2-3 weeks. This includes reading the entire document, consulting with a franchise attorney, contacting franchisees, and researching the franchisor. Rushing this process is how franchisees end up in bad deals.

**Q: What if the franchisor won't provide the FDD?**

A: Run. Federal law requires FDD disclosure 14 days before you sign or pay anything. A franchisor refusing to provide it is violating the law and represents unacceptable legal risk.

**Q: Can I trust the franchisee list in Item 23?**

A: Franchisors typically provide a curated list. Contact those listed, but also ask those franchisees for references to others (especially unhappy ones). If multiple franchisees won't return calls or refuse to talk, that's a red flag.

**Q: What's a typical failure rate for franchises?**

A: About 10% of franchises fail or close annually. Item 22 data shows your specific franchisor's rate. If it exceeds 15%, that's above average and worth investigating.

**Q: How do I know if Item 19 numbers are realistic?**

A: Contact franchisees and ask about their actual revenue and profitability. Ask specifically whether they've reached the Item 19 benchmarks. If most franchisees fall below Item 19 numbers, the representations may be inflated or based on outliers.

## Conclusion

The Franchise Disclosure Document is your roadmap to understanding franchise economics, franchisor stability, and realistic expectations for profitability. The FDD isn't perfect—it doesn't prevent all fraud or guarantee success—but it dramatically reduces your risk if you read it thoroughly and take time to verify key claims with current franchisees. Indy Clover's FDD, for example, clearly discloses the booth-rental model economics and support systems. Salad House's FDD provides Item 19 data demonstrating fast-casual unit economics in the health segment. By mastering all 23 items, especially Item 19's financial performance data, you transform from an uninformed buyer to an informed investor capable of distinguishing genuine opportunities from risky ones.
