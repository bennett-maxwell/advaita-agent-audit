# Franchise Resale vs. New Unit: Which Should You Buy in 2026?

When most people think about buying a franchise, they picture signing a new agreement and opening fresh. But there's another path that often gets overlooked: buying an existing franchise from a current owner.

Both options have real advantages — and real risks. Here's the full comparison.

## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale is when you purchase an existing, operating franchise location from a current franchisee rather than from the franchisor directly.

The seller might be:
- Retiring or relocating
- Moving on to another business venture
- Facing personal circumstances (health, family)
- Portfolio-pruning a multi-unit owner

## Side-by-Side Comparison

| Factor | New Unit | Resale |
|--------|----------|--------|
| Day-1 revenue | $0 | Existing cash flow |
| Brand recognition | Building | Already established |
| Customer base | None | Existing customers |
| Staff | Must hire | Often comes with team |
| Ramp-up time | 6-18 months | Often immediate |
| Certainty of numbers | Projections only | Historical P&L available |
| Upfront cost | Lower franchise fee | Often higher total price |
| Territory | You choose | Fixed (current location) |
| Problems inherited | None | Potentially some |

## When a Resale Makes More Sense

**You want cash flow from day one.** A performing resale location generates revenue immediately. No 6-12 month ramp-up. No waiting to build customer awareness.

**You're risk-averse.** You can review 2-3 years of actual financial statements. No projections or "what the brand says" — real numbers from this specific location.

**You're buying in a competitive market.** In dense areas, new territories may not exist. A resale is your only way in.

**You want existing staff.** Hiring and training is one of the hardest parts of launching. A resale often comes with an experienced team.

## When a New Unit Makes More Sense

**You want to choose your territory.** A resale locks you into an existing location. A new unit lets you pick the market.

**You want to build something your way.** New units let you implement systems from scratch without overriding existing habits.

**The resale price doesn't make sense.** Sellers often price based on emotional value, not financial reality. If the multiple is too high, new unit wins.

**The brand is early-stage with low recognition.** In young brands, a new unit in a good territory often outperforms an existing unit in a worse location.

## How Resales Are Priced

Most franchise resales are priced at 2-4x seller's discretionary earnings (SDE). A location generating $100,000/year in owner benefit would typically sell for $200,000-$400,000.

Add the franchise transfer fee (typically $5,000-$20,000) and any real estate/lease transfer costs.

**Due diligence items for resale:**
1. 3 years of tax returns (business)
2. Month-by-month P&L for the last 24 months
3. Lease terms and renewal options
4. Equipment age and condition
5. Reason for sale (verify independently)
6. Employee retention likelihood

## The Hybrid Approach

Some brands offer "resale development agreements" where you buy an existing unit AND have rights to open additional new units in the territory. This combines the immediate cash flow of a resale with the upside of expansion.

Ask your broker whether this option is available.

## Bottom Line

Resales are underutilized by first-time franchise buyers who don't know they exist. Before defaulting to a new unit, ask your franchisor or broker what resale opportunities are available in your target markets.

The right answer depends on your capital, risk tolerance, timeline, and territory preferences.
