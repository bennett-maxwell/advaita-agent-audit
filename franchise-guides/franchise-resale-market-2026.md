# Franchise Resale Market 2026: Buying an Existing Franchise Location

*By FranchiseKI | Updated May 2026 | franchiseki.com*

---

Most people think of buying a franchise as opening a brand-new location. But there's another path: buying an existing franchise location from the current owner. This is called a franchise resale, and in many cases it's a smarter entry point.

Here's what you need to know about the franchise resale market in 2026.

## What Is a Franchise Resale?

A franchise resale is when an existing franchisee sells their operating business to a new owner. You're buying:
- The franchise rights (with franchisor approval)
- The physical location and equipment
- Existing customers and revenue
- Trained staff (typically)
- The owner's remaining franchise term

You're NOT starting from zero. The location is already open, staff are already trained, and customers already come in the door.

## Why Owners Sell

Understanding the seller's motivation is critical. Legitimate reasons to sell:

- **Retirement** — especially from founding franchisees who opened in early brand growth
- **Relocation** — moving out of territory
- **Health or family circumstances** — personal situation changed
- **Capital redeployment** — rolling proceeds into multi-unit expansion elsewhere
- **Portfolio pruning** — multi-unit operator selling underperforming location

**Red flag:** Seller urgency with no clear reason. A business with a 3-year declining revenue trend. Operational issues the seller is trying to transfer to you.

## Resale vs. New Franchise: The Trade-offs

| Factor | New Location | Resale |
|--------|-------------|--------|
| Entry cost | Lower (equipment new, no goodwill) | Higher (paying for established revenue) |
| Revenue at opening | $0 | Existing (immediate cash flow) |
| Staff | Must hire and train | Usually inherits trained staff |
| Customer base | Must build from scratch | Already established |
| Unknowns | Location risk, ramp-up timeline | Historical data available |
| Franchisor support | Strong (important to them) | Variable (transaction-based) |
| Valuation transparency | Clear (Item 7 cost range) | Negotiated (seller's asking price) |

For buyers who need immediate cash flow or can't afford a 12-18 month ramp-up, resales often make more sense.

## How Franchise Resales Are Priced

Unlike a new franchise, resale pricing is negotiated — not fixed. Common valuation methods:

**1. Multiple of discretionary earnings (SDE):** Most common. Take annual seller discretionary earnings (revenue minus all expenses except owner's compensation and depreciation). Multiply by 2-3x for small service businesses, 3-5x for established retail/food concepts.

**2. Multiple of EBITDA:** Used for larger transactions ($500K+ EBITDA). Typically 4-7x.

**3. Asset-based:** Sum of tangible assets (equipment, inventory, leasehold improvements) at fair market value, often used when the business has minimal profit.

**Example:** A Sandwich franchise location earning $80,000 SDE annually might sell for $160,000–$240,000 (2-3x SDE). Add the new franchise fee (some franchisors waive for resales), and total acquisition cost is $175K–$275K.

## The Resale Process

**Step 1 — Find listings.** Franchise resales are listed through:
- Franchise brokers (like FranchiseKI)
- BizBuySell.com
- FranchiseGator resale section
- Direct from the franchisor's internal transfer list

**Step 2 — NDA + financials.** Sign a non-disclosure agreement, then review 3 years of P&L, tax returns, and lease documents.

**Step 3 — Franchisor approval.** The franchisor must approve any transfer. They evaluate you as a new franchisee — just like a new application. Transfer fees typically $5,000–$15,000.

**Step 4 — Due diligence.** Hire a business broker/accountant to verify financial statements. Call existing franchisees about the system. Review the lease remaining term.

**Step 5 — Negotiate purchase price and terms.** Common structures: lump sum, seller financing (seller carries a note for part of the purchase price), or SBA loan (SBA 7(a) and 504 both eligible for franchise resales).

**Step 6 — Training.** Most franchisors require new owners to complete initial training even when buying a resale.

## Key Due Diligence Questions for Resales

1. Why is the owner really selling? (Ask independently — not just the seller)
2. Is the lease transferable, and how many years remain?
3. What is the revenue trend over the last 3 years?
4. Are there any outstanding vendor debts, back royalties, or lease arrears?
5. What is the staff tenure? Would they stay with a new owner?
6. Has the franchisor cited this location for any compliance issues?
