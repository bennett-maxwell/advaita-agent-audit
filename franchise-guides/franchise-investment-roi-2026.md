## The Traditional Franchise Matching Process and Its Limitations

For decades, franchise matching relied on human brokers and franchisee candidates' personal research. This process suffered from several fundamental limitations that systematically produced suboptimal matches and prevented thorough due diligence.

**Limited Information Processing**: A diligent franchisee candidate might research 10-20 franchises before making a decision. This sample size captures perhaps 1-2% of available franchise opportunities. Most franchisees never discover the most suitable concepts because they're unable to systematically evaluate the full franchise market.

**Broker Incentive Misalignment**: Franchise brokers earn commissions based on which franchises they place, not on franchisee success. This incentive structure encourages brokers to recommend concepts with the highest commissions rather than those best aligned with franchisee capabilities and goals. While many brokers are genuinely ethical, structural incentives systematically bias recommendations toward brokerage interests.

**Surface-Level Analysis**: Traditional due diligence relies heavily on reviewing Franchise Disclosure Documents and conducting limited franchisee reference calls. This approach captures headline information but misses nuanced patterns—Which franchisees succeed despite challenging demographics? Which concepts systematically fail when operated part-time? What operational characteristics distinguish 90th percentile franchisees from 50th percentile performers?

**Cognitive Biases**: Human franchisee candidates are subject to numerous cognitive biases—confirmation bias (seeking information confirming preexisting beliefs), availability bias (overweighting recent examples), and anchoring bias (fixating on initial impressions). These biases systematically distort franchise evaluation.

**Time Constraints**: Thorough franchise due diligence is time-intensive. Reviewing comprehensive FDD documents, analyzing Item 19 financial data, conducting 20+ franchisee reference calls, and evaluating competitive positioning is a part-time job extending over months. Many franchisee candidates lack time for genuinely thorough evaluation.

These limitations combined mean that traditional franchise matching frequently produces franchisee-concept mismatches where the investment fails despite the concept's inherent viability, or where franchisees select mediocre concepts simply because they weren't aware of superior alternatives.
