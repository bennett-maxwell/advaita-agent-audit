# Franchise Royalty Fees Explained: What You Actually Pay in 2026

Royalty fees are the most misunderstood line item in franchise economics. Prospective buyers focus on the franchise fee — that upfront number that gets all the attention in every brand overview and investment summary. But the royalty is the ongoing obligation that will define your financial relationship with the franchisor for the entire life of your agreement.

This guide explains exactly how royalty fees work, what you should expect to pay, how to evaluate whether a royalty structure is reasonable for a given business model, and how tools like FranchiseScore can help you make an apples-to-apples comparison across brands.

---

## How Royalty Fees Work: The Basics

A royalty fee is the ongoing payment a franchisee makes to the franchisor in exchange for the right to operate under the brand's system, trademarks, and operational support infrastructure.

**The key characteristic of most royalty structures: royalties are calculated as a percentage of gross sales — not net profit.**

This distinction is critical. Your royalty obligation does not care whether you had a good month or a bad month. If your gross sales are $100,000 and your royalty rate is 6%, you owe $6,000 — regardless of whether you made $20,000 in profit or broke even or lost money.

This is why understanding the royalty in context of your expected unit economics is so important. A 6% royalty on a high-margin service business is very different from a 6% royalty on a low-margin retail or food service operation.

## Typical Royalty Rate Ranges in 2026

Royalty rates across the franchise industry cluster within a predictable range, with significant variation by category:

| Category | Typical Royalty Range |
|----------|----------------------|
| Food / QSR | 4% – 8% |
| Fitness & Wellness | 5% – 7% |
| Home Services | 5% – 8% |
| Commercial Services (B2B) | 6% – 10% |
| Retail | 4% – 6% |
| Education / Tutoring | 8% – 10% |
| Senior Care | 4% – 7% |
| Technology / Digital Services | 6% – 12% |
| Staffing / Recruiting | 6% – 8% |

**The industry-wide average is approximately 5% – 7%**, with outliers in both directions. A royalty below 4% is rare and worth scrutinizing (it may indicate the franchisor relies on product markups instead). A royalty above 10% requires a very strong business case supported by Item 19 financial data.

## Fixed vs. Percentage Royalties

While percentage-of-gross is the dominant royalty structure, some franchisors use alternative models:

**Flat-fee royalties:** A fixed weekly or monthly dollar amount regardless of sales volume. More predictable for owners, but potentially punishing in slow periods. Example: $1,500/week regardless of revenue.

**Sliding scale royalties:** The royalty percentage decreases as revenue increases. Example: 7% on the first $500K in annual sales, 5% on everything above $500K. Rewards growth.

**Revenue-tiered royalties:** Different rates apply based on unit age, market size, or volume thresholds. Common in newer franchise systems trying to attract early franchisees.

**Product-margin royalties:** The franchisor captures margin through required product purchases rather than a separate royalty fee. Common in food systems. These are functionally royalties even if not labeled as such.

When evaluating any franchise brand, identify the total royalty burden — including both the stated royalty rate and any required product or supply margins.

## Marketing / Advertising Funds: The Other Fee You Pay

In addition to royalties, virtually every franchise system charges a **marketing fee** or **advertising fund contribution**. This is a separate percentage of gross sales, dedicated to brand-level marketing that benefits the entire system.

**Typical marketing fund ranges:** 1% – 4% of gross sales

When you add marketing fees to royalties, the combined burden looks more like this:

| Component | Typical Range |
|-----------|--------------|
| Royalty | 5% – 8% |
| Marketing Fund | 1% – 3% |
| **Total Brand Fees** | **6% – 11%** |

For a unit generating $800,000 in annual revenue, a 9% combined fee burden (7% royalty + 2% marketing) means **$72,000 per year flowing to the franchisor** before you pay rent, labor, cost of goods, or any other operating expense.

This math is why your Item 19 financial data and your royalty structure must be evaluated together — not separately.

## How to Evaluate Royalty Burden: The Right Framework

The question is never "is 6% high or low?" The question is always "what does 6% look like against the unit economics this system produces?"

### Step 1: Pull the Item 19 Data

Item 19 of the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) is the only financial performance representation a franchisor is permitted to make to prospective franchisees. It may include:

- Gross revenue by percentile or average
- Net income or EBITDA (ideally)
- Revenue by territory size, unit age, or ownership type
- Same-store sales growth trends

Not all franchisors include robust Item 19 data. Some show only gross revenue ranges. Those that show net income or EBITDA give you a direct view of what's left after royalties, marketing fees, and operating costs.

### Step 2: Calculate Royalty as Percentage of Net

If a brand reports median gross revenue of $750,000 and median net income of $90,000, and charges a 6% royalty + 2% marketing fund:

- Combined fees: $750,000 × 8% = $60,000
- Net income after fees (already baked in if calculated correctly): $90,000
- Royalty as percentage of net: $60,000 / ($90,000 + $60,000) = 40% of gross profit

That 40% figure is what tells you whether the royalty is reasonable. If the system generates strong enough margins to support a 40% fee burden and still return meaningful income to owners, the royalty is defensible. If margins are thin and the 40% burden is destroying owner income, it's not.

### Step 3: Compare Across Brands

The only way to know if a royalty is competitive is to compare it against similar brands with similar unit economics. A 7% royalty on a business model that produces 30% EBITDA margins is a very different deal than a 5% royalty on a model that produces 12% EBITDA margins.

## SBA Loans and Royalties: What Lenders See

One question that comes up frequently: do SBA lenders evaluate royalty fees when making franchise loan decisions?

Yes — but perhaps not how you expect.

SBA lenders do not have royalty rate caps or automatic disqualifiers based on royalty percentage. What they evaluate is the **viability of the projected cash flow** after all obligations — including royalties — are paid.

If your loan package shows:
- $800,000 projected annual revenue
- $64,000 in royalty/marketing fees (8% combined)
- $640,000 in other operating costs
- $96,000 in annual projected operating income
- $60,000 in annual debt service

A lender looks at a 1.6x debt service coverage ratio and says this loan is viable. The royalty fee is a cost in the model, not a separate underwriting factor.

The implication: brands with high royalty rates but strong Item 19 unit economics can still be very financeable. Brands with low royalty rates but weak unit economics may struggle to support SBA financing if the underlying numbers don't work.

## Royalty Rate Red Flags to Watch For

**No Item 19 or gross-only Item 19:** If the franchisor can't or won't show you net income or EBITDA data, you have no way to evaluate whether the royalty structure is sustainable. This is a significant due diligence gap.

**Royalty escalations tied to unit age:** Some agreements start with a lower rate (6%) and escalate after a set number of years (7% or 8%). Understand the full lifecycle of your royalty obligation, not just the opening rate.

**Broad marketing fund discretion:** If the FDD gives the franchisor nearly unlimited discretion over how marketing funds are spent, you have limited recourse if those funds are not generating meaningful value for your unit.

**Royalty on gross vs. net receipts:** Some brands define "gross revenue" very broadly — including gift cards, catering, or third-party delivery fees where your net margin is significantly lower. Understand exactly what is included in the royalty base.

**No most-favored-nation provisions for large operators:** Multi-unit operators sometimes negotiate reduced royalty rates. As a single-unit buyer, understand whether you're paying a higher effective rate than established franchisees in your system.

## What Good Royalty Structures Look Like

The best franchise royalty structures share several characteristics:

1. **Transparent Item 19 data** that shows franchisee net income after all fees
2. **Royalty burden under 35% of gross profit** for the median unit
3. **Marketing fund with clear accountability** — audited statements and demonstrable ROI for franchisees
4. **Stable rates** with limited escalation tied to performance rather than arbitrary timelines
5. **Franchisee advisory councils** with meaningful input into how marketing funds and system resources are deployed

These characteristics are not universal. They are earned by franchisors who build their systems to serve franchisee profitability — not just to extract maximum fees from a captive network.
