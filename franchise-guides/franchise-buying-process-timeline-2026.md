# The Complete Franchise Buying Timeline: What to Expect in 2026

Understanding the franchise buying timeline helps prospective franchisees plan their journey with confidence. Most franchise purchases take 90-180 days from initial inquiry to signing.

## Phase 1: Research and Discovery (Weeks 1-4)

The journey begins with broad research. Prospective buyers should:

- Identify industries and business models that align with their skills and goals
- Research franchise opportunities using platforms like MapKi (mapki.franchiseki.com)
- Understand investment requirements (liquid capital, net worth minimums)
- Connect with a franchise consultant who can guide the process at no cost

**Why a Franchise Consultant Matters:** Franchise consultants are paid by franchisors, making their services free to buyers. An experienced consultant narrows thousands of options to 3-5 vetted matches based on your financial profile, lifestyle goals, and risk tolerance.

## Phase 2: Initial Qualification (Weeks 2-6)

During this phase, buyers qualify themselves and begin qualifying brands:

- Complete a financial profile (liquid capital, total net worth, credit score)
- Schedule introductory calls with 2-4 franchise brands
- Review initial information packages and brand websites
- Understand royalty structures, territory terms, and franchise fee ranges

**Key Numbers:** Most franchise opportunities require $50,000-$150,000 in liquid capital. Total investment ranges typically span $100,000-$500,000 for most established franchises.

## Phase 3: Franchise Disclosure Document Review (Weeks 4-8)

The FDD is the most important document in the franchise buying process:

- Franchisors must provide the FDD at least 14 days before signing any agreement
- The FDD contains 23 items including financial performance data (Item 19)
- Hire a franchise attorney to review the FDD ($1,500-$3,000 investment)
- Focus on Item 21 (financial statements), Item 19 (performance data), and Item 20 (outlets)

**Red Flags:** High litigation history (Item 3), weak Item 19 data, excessive franchisee turnover (Item 20), and hidden fees buried in other sections.

## Phase 4: Validation (Weeks 6-10)

Validation means speaking directly with existing franchisees:

- Contact 10-20 current franchisees from the FDD contact list
- Ask about their actual earnings vs. Item 19 projections
- Ask what they know now that they wish they'd known before signing
- Ask whether they would buy again with today's knowledge

**This is the most valuable step most buyers skip.** Real franchisees provide unfiltered insight no sales presentation can match.

## Phase 5: Discovery Day (Weeks 8-12)

Discovery Day is an in-person visit to franchise headquarters:

- Meet the leadership team, training staff, and operations team
- See the support systems firsthand
- Evaluate corporate culture and alignment with your values
- Ask hard questions about territory protection, technology, and marketing support

After Discovery Day, franchisors typically ask for a decision within 1-2 weeks.

## Phase 6: Financing (Weeks 6-14, Runs Concurrent)

Financing should begin early because it takes time:

- **SBA 7(a) Loans:** Most common for franchises. Requires 10-30% down, rates tied to prime. SBA-approved franchises (like Indy Clover) have faster approval paths.
- **SBA 504 Loans:** Better for real estate-heavy franchises
- **ROBS:** Roll over retirement funds into a C-Corp that buys the franchise
- **Home Equity:** Commonly used for lower-investment franchises
- **Franchisor Financing:** Some brands offer internal financing programs

## Phase 7: Legal Review and Signing (Weeks 12-16)

Final steps before becoming a franchisee:

- Your franchise attorney reviews the Franchise Agreement (different from the FDD)
- Negotiate any amendments (franchisors rarely allow changes, but territory terms sometimes move)
- Establish your business entity (LLC or S-Corp recommended)
- Sign the Franchise Agreement and pay the franchise fee

## Phase 8: Training and Opening (Weeks 16-26+)

Post-signing preparation varies by brand:

- Initial training (1-6 weeks at corporate headquarters)
- Site selection assistance (retail/service businesses)
- Pre-opening marketing support
- Grand opening support from corporate team

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Rushing the timeline.** The 90-day minimum exists for a reason—use it.
2. **Skipping franchisee validation.** This is non-negotiable for serious buyers.
3. **Not hiring a franchise attorney.** Generic business attorneys miss franchise-specific risks.
4. **Over-relying on Item 19 projections.** These are averages; validate with real franchisees.
5. **Ignoring territory protections.** Encroachment is a leading source of franchisee disputes.
