# How to Buy a Franchise in 2026: A Step-by-Step Guide

## Step 1: Clarify What You Actually Want (Before Looking at Any Brand)

The #1 mistake franchise buyers make is starting with a brand instead of starting with themselves. Before you look at a single FDD, answer these questions:

- **How much capital do you have?** (Liquid — for example, retirement accounts you'd have to liquidate don't count)
- **How many hours per week do you want to work?** (Owner-operator vs. semi-absentee)
- **What's your income goal in year 2?** (Replace W2? Supplement it? Build equity?)
- **Do you want B2C or B2B?** (Working with consumers vs. businesses changes everything about the lifestyle)
- **What geographies work for you?** (Some franchises have territory restrictions by state)

## Step 2: Get a Franchise Consultant (It's Free)

Franchise brokers and consultants are paid by franchisors — for example, not by you. A qualified consultant will:
- Run a discovery profile to match you to brands that fit your capital, time, and goals
- Give you access to FDDs and franchisee contact lists
- Coach you through the buying process

**Warning:** Low-quality consultants have relationships with 3-5 brands and will only show you those. High-quality consultants have relationships with 100+ brands and use data to match.

## Step 3: Run the FDD (Franchise Disclosure Document) Correctly

The FDD is a 200-400 page legal document. Here's what actually matters:

- Item 5: Franchise fees (initial + ongoing royalties)
- Item 7: Estimated initial investment range
- Item 19: Financial Performance Representations (closest to projected revenue/profit)
- Item 20: Outlet count (how many opened, closed, transferred in last 3 years)
- Item 21: Audited financial statements of the franchisor

## Step 4: Talk to Franchisees (Not the Ones the Franchisor Picks)

Item 20 of the FDD includes a full list of current and former franchisees with contact info. Call 10-15 current owners. Ask:
- What did you NOT know going in that you wish you had known?
- Is the Item 19 representative of your experience?
- Would you buy again?

## Step 5: Validate the Territory

Before signing: understand your exclusive territory boundaries, what exclusive means (some franchises grant exclusivity on unit count, not ZIP code), and what the market data looks like for your location.

## Step 6: Get Franchise-Specific Legal Review

Have a franchise attorney review the FDD and franchise agreement before signing. Budget $1,500-$3,500.

## Step 7: Secure Financing

- SBA loans (7a and 504): Most common for franchises
- Franchisor financing: Some brands offer in-house financing
- ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups): Use 401K funds without early withdrawal penalty
