## Understanding the Language: Liquid Capital vs. Total Investment

Before diving into financing options, it is essential to understand two terms that appear in almost every franchise opportunity profile:

**Liquid capital** (sometimes called "cash required" or "minimum liquid assets") refers to the amount of money you need to have readily accessible — in cash, checking, savings, or money market accounts — without liquidating retirement accounts or selling assets. Most franchisors require liquid capital to demonstrate financial stability and fund early operating expenses that financing may not cover.

**Total investment** is the full estimated cost to open and operate the franchise through the initial ramp-up period. It includes the franchise fee, equipment, real estate buildout (if applicable), working capital, inventory, and all other startup costs listed in Item 7 of the Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD).

The gap between liquid capital and total investment is where financing lives. If a franchise requires $50,000 in liquid capital but the total investment is $200,000, that $150,000 gap is typically funded through one or more of the financing mechanisms described below.

## SBA 7(a) Loans: The Most Common Franchise Financing Tool

The Small Business Administration's 7(a) loan program is the backbone of franchise financing in the United States. SBA loans are not issued directly by the government — they are issued by approved lenders (banks, credit unions, CDFIs) and partially guaranteed by the SBA, which reduces the lender's risk and makes them willing to extend credit to business buyers who would not qualify for conventional commercial loans.

Key characteristics of SBA 7(a) loans for franchise buyers:

- **Loan amounts** up to $5 million, though most franchise transactions involve $150,000 to $750,000
- **Terms** of up to 10 years for working capital and equipment, up to 25 years for real estate
- **Interest rates** are variable, tied to the prime rate plus a lender spread, and are regulated by the SBA
- **Down payment** typically 10% to 20% of the total loan amount
- **SBA Franchise Registry.** The SBA maintains a registry of franchise brands whose FDDs have been pre-reviewed and approved for expedited SBA lending. Buying an SBA-approved franchise significantly streamlines the loan process.

## ROBS: Rollover for Business Startups

ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups) is a financing strategy that allows buyers to use retirement account funds — 401(k), IRA, or other qualified accounts — to invest in a franchise without triggering the early withdrawal penalty or income taxes that would normally apply.

The mechanism works as follows:
1. You establish a new C Corporation
2. The corporation establishes a qualified retirement plan
3. Your existing retirement funds are rolled into the new plan
4. The plan purchases stock in the new corporation
5. The corporation uses those funds to buy the franchise

ROBS is legal and has been used by tens of thousands of franchise buyers. However, it comes with complexity: the structure requires ongoing compliance, annual plan administration, and careful handling to avoid IRS scrutiny. Work only with a ROBS administrator who specializes in franchise transactions and has a strong compliance track record.

ROBS is particularly attractive for buyers who have substantial retirement savings but limited liquid cash — and who prefer not to take on debt.

## Home Equity Options: HELOCs and Cash-Out Refinancing

For homeowners with significant equity, tapping that equity to fund a franchise is a viable option — though it carries meaningful risk, since your home serves as collateral.

**HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit):** A revolving credit line secured by your home equity. Interest rates are typically lower than unsecured business loans. HELOCs offer flexibility — you draw only what you need, when you need it.

**Cash-out refinancing:** Replaces your existing mortgage with a larger one, with the difference paid to you in cash. Best suited when current mortgage rates are favorable relative to your existing rate.

The risk of both options is obvious: if the business underperforms, you are servicing home-secured debt. Franchise buyers who use home equity should have adequate working capital reserves and a realistic plan for the ramp-up period.

## Portfolio Loans and Margin Loans

For buyers with substantial investment portfolios, securities-backed lending offers another path. Portfolio loans (sometimes called margin loans or pledged asset lines) use brokerage accounts as collateral and typically offer:

- Lower interest rates than unsecured business lending
- No need to liquidate positions (and therefore no capital gains tax event)
- Flexible repayment terms

The risk here is also straightforward: if the market drops significantly, you may face a margin call that requires you to contribute additional collateral or repay a portion of the loan quickly.

Portfolio loans are best suited for buyers who have significant investments, prefer not to liquidate, and have the risk tolerance to maintain a leveraged position.

## How SBA-Approved Franchises Accelerate Financing

Not all franchise brands are created equal in the eyes of lenders. SBA-approved franchises — those listed on the SBA's Franchise Directory — have had their FDD and franchise agreement reviewed and pre-cleared by the SBA. This means:

- Lenders can skip much of the franchise-specific due diligence that slows non-approved deals
- Underwriting timelines are shorter
- Some lenders have preferred relationships with specific SBA-approved brands

When evaluating franchise opportunities, ask specifically whether the brand is SBA-approved. **Indy Clover's February 2026 SBA approval** is a concrete example of why this matters — buyers interested in that brand now have a cleaner path to SBA financing than they would have had in 2025.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: What credit score do I need to get an SBA loan for a franchise?**
Most SBA lenders look for a minimum credit score of 680 to 700, though some lenders will consider lower scores with compensating factors like strong collateral or a large down payment. Higher scores unlock better rates and faster approvals.

**Q: Can I use a ROBS and an SBA loan together?**
Yes. Combining ROBS (for the equity portion) with an SBA loan (for the debt portion) is a common strategy for buyers who have retirement savings but need additional leverage. This structure requires careful coordination between your ROBS administrator and your SBA lender.

**Q: What does "SBA-approved" mean for a franchise?**
It means the SBA has reviewed and pre-cleared the franchise's disclosure documents, allowing lenders to underwrite SBA loans for that brand more quickly and with less friction. Indy Clover received this designation in February 2026.

**Q: How much liquid capital do I actually need before starting the financing process?**
This varies by brand and financing path. As a general rule, having 20% to 30% of the total investment in liquid capital — plus adequate reserves for personal living expenses during the ramp-up — is a strong starting position.
