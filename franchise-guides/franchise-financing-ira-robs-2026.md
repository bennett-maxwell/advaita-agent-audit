# How to Use Your IRA or 401(k) to Buy a Franchise in 2026 (ROBS Explained)

*By FranchiseKI | Updated May 2026 | franchiseki.com*

---

Most people don't know that your retirement savings can fund a business purchase — without paying early withdrawal penalties or income taxes. The strategy is called Rollover for Business Startups (ROBS), and it's one of the most commonly used financing methods for franchise purchases.

Here's how it works, when it makes sense, and what to watch out for.

## What Is ROBS (Rollover for Business Startups)?

ROBS allows you to use funds from a qualified retirement account (401(k), traditional IRA, 403(b), etc.) to invest in a new business — specifically, as a shareholder in a C-Corporation that buys the franchise.

**Key benefit:** No early withdrawal penalty. No income tax on the rollover. The funds go directly into your business as equity.

**How it works (simplified):**
1. Create a new C-Corporation
2. Establish a 401(k) plan within that C-Corp
3. Roll your existing retirement funds into the new 401(k)
4. The 401(k) uses those funds to purchase stock in your C-Corp
5. Your C-Corp has cash to invest in the franchise

Done correctly, this is a fully IRS-compliant strategy. Done incorrectly, it can trigger massive penalties and back taxes.

## Who Qualifies for ROBS?

**You need:**
- At least $50,000 in qualified retirement accounts (most providers require $50K-$100K minimum)
- A traditional 401(k), traditional IRA, 403(b), TSP, or similar pre-tax account
- **Roth IRA funds do NOT qualify** (already post-tax)
- You must be the active operator of the business (not a passive investor)

**Common account sources:**
- Former employer 401(k) accounts (can be rolled into a new 401(k) without penalty at any age)
- Traditional IRAs
- Government TSP accounts (military, federal employees)
- 403(b) plans (teachers, non-profits)

## The Numbers: ROBS vs. SBA Loan

| Factor | ROBS | SBA 7(a) Loan |
|--------|------|----------------|
| Down payment required | None (full amount from retirement funds) | 10-30% of total investment |
| Interest cost | None | 7-10% annually (Prime + 2.25%) |
| Monthly debt payment | None | $1,500–$5,000/mo on $150K loan |
| Impact on personal credit | None | Credit check required |
| Early years cash flow | Better (no debt service) | Worse (loan payments) |
| Retirement account impact | Funds leave retirement | Retirement untouched |
| Setup complexity | High (requires specialist) | Moderate |
| Ongoing compliance | Annual reporting required | Standard loan covenants |

## ROBS Provider Costs

You need a specialized ROBS provider — not a general attorney or accountant. Leading providers:

- **Guidant Financial** — most recognized, thorough compliance support
- **Benetrends** — strong compliance track record
- **FranFund** — franchise-focused, often pairs with SBA

Typical costs: $5,000–$6,000 setup fee + $1,500–$2,500/year ongoing compliance.

**Important:** The ongoing compliance requirement is non-negotiable. The 401(k) plan requires annual Form 5500 filing, non-discrimination testing, and plan document updates. Skipping compliance is how ROBS arrangements get unwound by the IRS.

## Hybrid ROBS + SBA Strategy

Many franchisees combine ROBS with an SBA loan:
1. Use ROBS for your down payment (typically 20-30% of total investment)
2. Use SBA 7(a) loan for the remainder

**Why this works:** SBA requires 20-30% equity injection. ROBS satisfies that requirement without depleting all retirement funds. You preserve some retirement savings while minimizing debt service.

Example: $250K franchise investment
- ROBS: $75,000 (30% equity via retirement funds)
- SBA loan: $175,000 at 8.5% (~$1,750/month payment)

vs. full ROBS ($250,000 from retirement) with zero debt service.

## Risks to Understand

**1. Investment concentration.** You're putting retirement funds into a single business. If the business fails, those funds are gone. Diversification is eliminated.

**2. IRS scrutiny.** The IRS scrutinizes ROBS arrangements closely. Compliance is non-negotiable — not just best practice.

**3. You must be active.** ROBS requires you to be an active employee of the business. Passive investors don't qualify.

**4. C-Corp structure.** C-Corps face double taxation on dividends. Work with a tax advisor to structure profit distributions correctly.

**5. Fiduciary responsibility.** You become the trustee of a qualified plan. You have legal obligations to plan participants (you) that must be met.

## Is ROBS Right For You?

**ROBS works best when:**
- You have $100K+ in retirement accounts
- The franchise requires $150K-$500K total investment
- You want to minimize or eliminate monthly debt service
- You have the discipline for annual plan compliance
- You're under 50 and have time to rebuild retirement savings from business profits

**SBA may be better when:**
- Your retirement savings are under $50K
- You want to preserve retirement diversification
- You qualify for strong SBA terms (good credit, collateral)
