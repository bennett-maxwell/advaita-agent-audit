# Franchise Discovery Day Checklist 2026: 30 Questions to Ask Before You Sign

**Target keyword: franchise discovery day checklist**

Discovery Day is the pivotal moment in your franchise journey — the in-person visit where you meet the franchisor's team, see corporate operations, and make the final assessment before signing. Most buyers arrive underprepared. They ask surface-level questions, get sold by the energy of the experience, and sign without the intelligence they needed.

This checklist gives you 30 specific questions — organized by category — so you walk out of Discovery Day knowing exactly what you're getting into.

---

## Before You Go: Setting Yourself Up to Succeed

Discovery Day is not a courtesy visit. It is a business interview — and you are interviewing the franchisor, not the other way around.

**Prepare your financial baseline first.** Before Discovery Day, you should already have:
- Your FranchiseScore from a platform like [MapKi](https://mapki.franchiseki.com) or equivalent analysis
- Your FDD reviewed with a franchise attorney
- 5-10 franchisee validation calls completed
- A unit economics model built with your accountant

If you haven't done these things, you're not ready for Discovery Day. The questions in this checklist are most powerful when you already know what the documents say and are using Discovery Day to validate, probe, and read between the lines.

**Take notes.** Bring a notebook or tablet. Write down exact language, not just summaries. The specific words people use reveal a lot.

**Go with a clear decision framework.** You should know in advance: what would need to be true about this franchisor for me to sign? What would make me walk away? Clarity before you walk in keeps you from getting swept up in the energy of the day.

## Category 1: Financial Performance and Unit Economics (Questions 1-7)

These questions probe what you can actually expect to earn — the most important thing you need to know.

**Question 1: What does a unit in the bottom quartile of your system look like — what went wrong and what did they do about it?**

Why this matters: Franchisors always show you the top performers in marketing. You need to understand the range, especially the bottom. How they answer this tells you whether they take ownership of system performance or blame franchisees.

**Question 2: What's the typical timeline to break even for a new location in our market type?**

Why this matters: Break-even timelines are almost never in the marketing materials. Franchisors who know their system know this number. Vague answers like "it depends" without substantive follow-up are a warning sign.

**Question 3: Can you walk me through how a typical franchisee builds from $0 to their current run rate in year one?**

Why this matters: Forces a concrete operational and revenue narrative, not abstract projections. The story they tell reveals how well they understand their own franchisee journey.

**Question 4: Your Item 19 shows [X metric]. What percentage of active franchisees achieve that number or better?**

Why this matters: Forces them to contextualize the headline figure. If 80% of franchisees hit the Item 19 figure, that's very different from 30%.

**Question 5: What are the most common reasons franchisees in your system underperform — and what's the fix?**

Why this matters: Every franchise has known failure patterns. Franchisors who've been operating long enough know what they are. Willingness to name them honestly indicates a system that learns.

**Question 6: What does your royalty + marketing fee structure look like compared to what top-performing franchisees actually earn as a percentage of revenue?**

Why this matters: At a 6% royalty + 2% ad fund, you're paying 8% of gross off the top. In low-margin businesses, this can be the difference between profit and loss. Verify the math against Item 19 data.

**Question 7: Do you have any internal benchmarks (revenue per territory size, customers per unit, revenue per square foot) that indicate a unit is on-track in months 1-6?**

Why this matters: Leading indicators of success should be known and tracked. A franchisor who can tell you "at month 3, you should be at $X" has a well-understood system. One who can't is guessing.

## Category 2: Operations and Support (Questions 8-14)

These questions assess whether the franchisor's support infrastructure is real or a sales promise.

**Question 8: Who specifically will be my primary point of contact after I open — and what is their caseload?**

Why this matters: "Our franchise business consultants will support you" is meaningless. Ask for a name and ask how many franchisees that person supports. An FBC supporting 50+ units cannot give meaningful support to any of them.

**Question 9: Walk me through exactly what happens in my first 90 days — who calls me, what do they review, what triggers their involvement?**

Why this matters: Forces a specific operational answer. The 90-day plan reveals whether there's a structured onboarding process or loose improvisation.

**Question 10: What's your technology stack — what systems do I use daily, and what happens when they go down?**

Why this matters: POS systems, CRM platforms, scheduling software — you'll live in these daily. Understand whether they're proprietary or third-party, whether they're maintained, and what the support process is when something breaks during business hours.

**Question 11: What's changed about your support model in the last 24 months, and why?**

Why this matters: Systems that are improving tell you what they changed and why. Systems that haven't changed — or can't articulate change — may be stagnant.

**Question 12: What marketing support do you provide at the local level versus national? What can I customize versus what is mandated?**

Why this matters: National brand fund money is supposed to drive awareness that benefits franchisees. How it's allocated, who controls it, and what the ROI has been are critical questions — especially in year one when you need local customer acquisition.

**Question 13: How do you handle it when a franchisee is struggling — what's your intervention process?**

Why this matters: Performance intervention reveals whether the franchisor is a partner or an enforcement organization. Are they investing in turnaround, or are they processing violations?

**Question 14: What's your franchisee satisfaction survey score, and how is it measured?**

Why this matters: Serious franchisors measure satisfaction. FBR (Franchise Business Review) and similar third parties conduct independent surveys. Ask if they use third-party measurement. If they don't measure satisfaction, ask why.

## Category 3: System Health and Growth (Questions 15-20)

These questions assess whether the franchise system is growing or contracting — and what the future looks like.

**Question 15: How many units have opened, closed, transferred, and been terminated in each of the last three years?**

Why this matters: Item 20 gives you some of this, but the conversation reveals tone. High termination rates relative to openings indicate a system under stress.

**Question 16: What's your target unit count and timeline to get there — and how does that benefit existing franchisees?**

Why this matters: Aggressive growth is great for the franchisor's enterprise value. It's not always good for existing franchisees who may see territory encroachment or diluted support resources. Probe the connection.

**Question 17: Who are your competitors, and how do you differentiate — and how has that differentiation held up in the last 12 months?**

Why this matters: Every franchisor will tell you why they're better. But do they know the competitive landscape accurately? Do they have specific examples of winning against competitors?

**Question 18: Have you made any major changes to the business model, product line, or concept in the last 3 years — and how were franchisees notified and compensated?**

Why this matters: Model changes are inevitable. How they're handled (imposed vs. collaborative, compensated vs. not) tells you about the franchisor-franchisee relationship fundamentally.

**Question 19: Is the company independently owned, private equity-backed, or publicly held — and what does that mean for decision-making and future changes?**

Why this matters: PE-backed franchisors operate with specific exit timelines and financial pressures that affect franchisees. Knowing the ownership structure helps you understand whose interests are being optimized.

**Question 20: What's the renewal rate when franchisees reach the end of their initial term — and what typically determines whether a franchisee renews?**

Why this matters: If franchisees aren't renewing, they're voting with their feet. Low renewal rates are one of the most objective indicators of franchisee health.

## Category 4: Legal and Agreement Terms (Questions 21-25)

These questions probe the terms you'll be living under — with a franchise attorney's guidance.

**Question 21: What provisions in the franchise agreement have been negotiated or modified for franchisees in the past two years?**

Why this matters: If the answer is "nothing is negotiable," that's fine — many systems operate this way — but you need to know upfront. If they say some things are negotiable but won't tell you what, probe further.

**Question 22: What are the conditions under which you can terminate my franchise agreement, and has that authority ever been exercised in the past 5 years?**

Why this matters: Item 17 of the FDD covers termination. Have your attorney review it. But Discovery Day gives you the human context — how do they use this power? Have they used it aggressively?

**Question 23: What happens to my investment if you sell the company or merge with another brand?**

Why this matters: Buyouts and mergers are increasingly common. Your agreement may transfer to a new parent company with different values, support models, or cost structures. Understand your rights if this happens.

**Question 24: What are the in-term and post-term non-compete provisions — specifically, what am I restricted from doing after my franchise ends?**

Why this matters: Non-competes vary significantly. Some prevent you from working in adjacent industries for 2 years within 50+ miles. This affects your exit options. Know exactly what you're agreeing to.

**Question 25: If I want to sell my franchise, what is your right of first refusal — and how has that worked in practice?**

Why this matters: Most franchise agreements include the franchisor's right to match any offer you receive when selling. In practice, exercising this right can delay or complicate your exit. Ask for specific examples.

## Category 5: Culture and Relationship (Questions 26-30)

These questions assess the intangibles — because you're entering a 10-year relationship.

**Question 26: How would your most successful franchisees describe your relationship with them in one sentence?**

Why this matters: Forces them to articulate the relationship from the franchisee's perspective. If they struggle or give a corporate non-answer, that's informative.

**Question 27: What's the biggest mistake you've made as a franchisor, and what did you learn from it?**

Why this matters: Intellectual honesty about failures is a strong cultural signal. Franchisors who can't acknowledge mistakes or who attribute all problems externally tend to have adversarial franchisee relationships.

**Question 28: What qualities do your most successful franchisees share — and where have you seen people who looked great on paper underperform?**

Why this matters: Knowing what makes franchisees successful and unsuccessful helps you self-assess honestly. It also reveals whether the franchisor has studied their own system.

**Question 29: How do franchisees have a voice in system changes — is there a franchisee advisory council, and how much influence does it actually have?**

Why this matters: A franchisee advisory council (FAC) is a healthy system signal. An FAC that's only consultative vs. one that has real input into fee changes, product development, and operational requirements tells you a lot about the power dynamic.

**Question 30: If I get 18 months in and I'm struggling — what does our conversation look like and what resources can you deploy?**

Why this matters: This is the most important scenario question you can ask. How they answer tells you whether they're a partner or an adversarial counterparty when things get hard.

## How to Analyze Discovery Day Answers

After Discovery Day, evaluate what you heard against three filters:

**Filter 1: Specificity vs. generality.**
Vague answers to operational questions ("we really support our franchisees!") should concern you more than specific, nuanced answers that include some acknowledgment of challenges. Specificity indicates knowledge of the system. Vagueness often masks either ignorance or intentional avoidance.

**Filter 2: Franchisee validation alignment.**
Do the answers you got at Discovery Day match what franchisees told you during validation calls? If franchisees said support is understaffed and corporate said "we have the best support team in the industry" — one of them is wrong. Assume the franchisees.

**Filter 3: Your gut, calibrated.**
Discovery Day is designed to make you feel good. There will be a nice meal, enthusiastic people, a polished facility. Separate the experience from the data. Ask yourself: if this visit had been conducted over Zoom with just Q&A, would I feel the same?

---

## Red Flags That Should Make You Pause (or Walk Away)

- Inability to name or explain Item 19 without deflection
- Answers that contradict what franchisees told you in validation
- Pressure tactics around territory availability or training cohort timing
- Vague or defensive responses to the bottom-quartile question
- A franchisee advisory council that has no actual influence
- Termination rate that's more than 10% of system size per year
- No systematic way to measure or report franchisee satisfaction
- Legal team present at Discovery Day for no explained reason
- Leadership turnover at senior support levels in the past 18 months
